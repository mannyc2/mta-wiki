Overview

The permanent 14th Street Busway is the post-pilot version of NYC DOT's 14th Street Transit and Truck Priority design. DOT describes 14th Street as a connector between the Lower East Side, Union Square, and the Meatpacking District, and frames the busway around moving people and goods safely and reliably while preserving local access. The pilot began on October 3, 2019, after M14A/D Select Bus Service began on July 1, 2019; DOT says the busway was made permanent in June 2020, with a plan to extend bus lanes east. [14th_street_busway#p001_b0001]

Operating Design

From 6 AM to 10 PM, only buses, trucks, and emergency vehicles may make through trips on 14th Street between 3rd Avenue and 9th Avenue. Other vehicles may make local trips to businesses, residences, garages, the curb, pickups, and dropoffs, but must turn at the next available right; from 10 PM to 6 AM, all vehicles may make through trips. The busway also uses short-term metered commercial loading zones, a general ban on left turns off 14th Street except for MTA buses at signed locations, automated cameras, and NYPD traffic-agent enforcement. [14th_street_busway#p001_b0001]

The project is tied directly to M14A/D SBS operations: DOT says the corridor serves about 28,000 daily M14 riders and combines blocks of exclusive access with standard bus lanes to provide bus priority from 9th Avenue to 1st Avenue. DOT also explains the truck allowance as part of 14th Street's designated truck-route role. [14th_street_busway#p001_b0001]

Program Context And Reported Effects

A later Comptroller report places the project in the Better Buses policy lineage: the Better Buses Action Plan set goals for a 25% citywide bus-speed increase, 10-15 miles of new bus lanes per year, upgrades to existing bus lanes, TSP, busways, and automated enforcement; the report then describes Better Buses Restart as installing major bus lanes and busways while extending and making permanent 14th Street and planning additional busways. [behind_schedule_2025#p023_c0002] [behind_schedule_2025#p023_c0003]

DOT's own busway page reports that the pilot increased bus speeds by as much as 24% and ridership by as much as 30%; IBO repeats those figures as the cited rationale for making the pilot permanent and calls 14th Street a prime local example of converting a busy commercial corridor to a busway. [14th_street_busway#p001_b0001] [speeding_up_slowly_2025#p007_c0006]

A separate Comptroller summary gives an even stronger travel-speed figure, stating that travel speeds on 14th Street increased up to 47% after the dedicated busway was installed in 2019, making corridor bus trips 10 minutes faster. Treat the 24%, 30%, and 47% figures as reported benefit claims from different summaries, not as a single reconciled metric series. [behind_schedule_2025#p025_c0005]

How To Read It

This page is strongest as a permanent-rule and case-study page: it records the operating restrictions, permanence date, M14A/D SBS connection, and reported benefits. It should not be read as proof that citywide bus-priority goals were achieved. The Comptroller report credits bus-priority programs with tangible improvements, including four dedicated busways and widespread TSP, but also says bus performance stagnated and rider improvements remained undelivered. IBO similarly found peak weekday bus speeds across 316 routes were 7.6 mph in December 2024, close to 7.8 mph in January 2019, with SBS speeds listed at 8.6 mph in December 2024 versus 8.9 mph in January 2019. [behind_schedule_2025#p004_c0005] [behind_schedule_2025#p004_c0006] [speeding_up_slowly_2025#p007_c0010] [speeding_up_slowly_2025#p008_c0006]
