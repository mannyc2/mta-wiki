project_2017-street-improvement-79th

Scope Note

This record currently brings together two source-backed uses of the same generic project name. In the 79th Street source, the 2017 Street Improvement Project is the M79 SBS launch package on the 79th Street transit corridor; in the Woodhaven Boulevard sources, the same label refers to the Q52/Q53 SBS launch work on Woodhaven Boulevard. Treat the page as a mixed 79th Street/Woodhaven record unless the canonical identity is split or reviewed. 79th Street / M79 SBS

For 79th Street, NYC DOT describes the 2017 project as the launch of M79 Select Bus Service replacing the M79 Local. The package included 8 bus queue jump lanes, one full-block bus lane, signal timing improvements, off-board fare payment, real-time passenger information, and bus stop amenities/street furniture. [79_st_riverside_dr_fdr_dr_cb8_apr2025#p005_c0002]

The same 2025 CB8 presentation frames 79th Street as a major M79-SBS corridor with 13,500 daily riders, connections to 4 subway lines and 16 bus routes, and a recent safety context of 26 people killed or severely injured over 2020-2024. [79_st_riverside_dr_fdr_dr_cb8_apr2025#p004_c0003] Reported post-launch operating outcomes include 6% less bus bunching, 21% lower dwell time at stops, 9% first-year ridership growth, 4%-8% lower car travel times, 8% average bus speed improvement, and up to 45% fewer late buses. [79_st_riverside_dr_fdr_dr_cb8_apr2025#p007_c0002]

DOT also reports safety improvements when comparing the 2012-2016 average with the 2017-2024 average: pedestrian injuries down 63%, cyclist injuries down 17%, motor-vehicle crashes down 62%, and total incidents down 57%, with the safety table attributed to NYPD and NYS data. [79_st_riverside_dr_fdr_dr_cb8_apr2025#p008_c0004] [79_st_riverside_dr_fdr_dr_cb8_apr2025#p008_c0005] [79_st_riverside_dr_fdr_dr_cb8_apr2025#p008_c0007]

The 79th Street source is also clear that the 2017 in-house project left follow-up needs: some improvements could not be completed with in-house materials, pedestrian safety measures needed permanent materials to have full impact, some stops remained crowded or circulation-constrained, some bus operations issues remained, and sections of roadway and sidewalk still needed capital repair. [79_st_riverside_dr_fdr_dr_cb8_apr2025#p009_c0002]

Woodhaven Boulevard / Q52-Q53 SBS

For Woodhaven Boulevard, the 2024 CB10 and CB6 sources describe a 2017 Street Improvement Project tied to the Q52/Q53 SBS launch. The project installed 6.85 miles of bus lanes: 3.95 miles of offset alignment from Queens Boulevard to Park Lane South, 1.53 miles of median alignment from Park Lane South to Rockaway Boulevard, and 1.37 miles of curbside alignment from Rockaway Boulevard to North Conduit Avenue. [capital_woodhaven_blvd_capital_projects_cb10_dec2024#p005_c0002] [capital_woodhaven_blvd_capital_projects_cb6_sept2024#p005_c0002]

The Woodhaven package also included bus boarding islands for 12 bus stops, described as creating one of New York City's first median-running bus corridors; later slides add bus stop amenities, pedestrian safety measures at 14 intersections, and refurbished medians with new ADA-compliant pedestrian refuges at 11 intersections. [capital_woodhaven_blvd_capital_projects_cb10_dec2024#p006_c0003] [capital_woodhaven_blvd_capital_projects_cb10_dec2024#p008_c0003] [capital_woodhaven_blvd_capital_projects_cb10_dec2024#p009_c0003]

The corridor context is larger than the project itself: DOT describes Woodhaven Boulevard as a major Q52/Q53 SBS corridor with 30,000+ daily riders, connections to Queens Boulevard and the Rockaways, 8 subway lines, 20 local and express bus routes, and Vision Zero priority status since 2019. [capital_woodhaven_blvd_capital_projects_cb10_dec2024#p004_c0002] Reported operating outcomes include average bus travel-time improvement of 3 minutes in each direction, a 10% improvement, up-to-80% on-time-performance improvement, and northbound Q52/Q53 late arrivals shrinking from 52% of the time to 10% of the time in the 2019-vs-2017 comparison. [capital_woodhaven_blvd_capital_projects_cb10_dec2024#p011_c0002]

As with 79th Street, the Woodhaven sources present the 2017 SIP as a foundation rather than a finished capital condition. Remaining issues included temporary pedestrian safety materials, crowding and lack of amenities at stops, poor roadway and median conditions, and some bus operations issues. The later capital-project toolkit aimed to make temporary safety work permanent, add more pedestrian safety measures, create more circulation space and amenities at bus stops, and add trees and landscape elements. [capital_woodhaven_blvd_capital_projects_cb10_dec2024#p013_c0002] [capital_woodhaven_blvd_capital_projects_cb10_dec2024#p014_c0002]
