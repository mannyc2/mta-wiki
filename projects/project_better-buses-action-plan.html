Better Buses Action Plan

project_better-buses-action-plan

Overview

The Better Buses Action Plan was NYC DOT's 2019 bus-priority plan, organized around a headline target to raise average citywide bus speeds by 25% by the end of 2020. The plan used an 8.0 mph citywide bus-speed baseline and set a 10.0 mph target, based on MTA Bus Time GPS speed calculations. [better_buses_action_plan_2019#p001_c0001] [better_buses_action_plan_2019#p009_c0002] [better_buses_action_plan_2019#p009_c0014]

The plan paired that speed target with annual delivery goals: improve 5 miles of existing bus lanes, install 10-15 miles of new bus lanes, pilot up to 2 miles of physically separated bus lanes in 2019, add 300 Transit Signal Priority intersections per year, expand camera and NYPD bus-lane enforcement, support MTA borough bus network redesigns, and press for all-door boarding and a restart of SBS-style operating improvements. [better_buses_action_plan_2019#p009_c0004] [better_buses_action_plan_2019#p009_c0006] [better_buses_action_plan_2019#p009_c0008] [better_buses_action_plan_2019#p009_c0012]

Project Portfolio

The 2019 project portfolio was explicitly tied to the action-plan goals: DOT described its bus-priority street design projects as a way to increase speeds, improve bus-stop accessibility, and support Vision Zero on bus corridors. The plan mapped 24 project locations across all five boroughs, including Manhattan corridors such as Battery Place and 42nd Street, Bronx locations such as Baychester Avenue, Broadway, Mosholu Parkway, and Webster Avenue, Queens locations including Woodhaven Boulevard, Broadway, Main Street, Union Turnpike, Fresh Pond Road, and Rockaway Beach Boulevard, Brooklyn corridors including Pennsylvania Avenue, Livingston Street, Malcolm X Boulevard, Church Avenue, and East New York Avenue, and Narrows Road on Staten Island. [better_buses_action_plan_2019#p017_c0002] [better_buses_action_plan_2019#p023_c0002] [better_buses_action_plan_2019#p023_c0003] [better_buses_action_plan_2019#p023_c0004] [better_buses_action_plan_2019#p023_c0005] [better_buses_action_plan_2019#p023_c0006]

Later project decks continued to use the action plan as the program frame. Battery Place and 42nd Street decks restated the Mayor's 2019 State of the City target, the April 2019 release, and the 24 priority projects; project-specific slides identify Battery Place, Fresh Pond Road, and Church Avenue as 2019 priority projects because of slow speeds, high ridership or bus volumes, and unreliable travel times. [battery_pl_oct2019#p002_c0002] [battery_pl_oct2019#p003_c0002] [42nd_st_cb5_jun242019#p004_c0002] [fresh_pond_rd_may2019#p005_c0002] [church_ave_public_forum_jul2019#p005_c0002]

2020 And Later Thread

The action plan anticipated post-2019 bus-priority work being coordinated with MTA borough bus network redesigns, starting with the Bronx, and called for borough-specific Better Buses action plans for 2020 and beyond. [better_buses_action_plan_2019#p018_c0002] A Bronx CB5 presentation later described DOT analysis of 46 major Bronx corridors through the Bronx Bus Network Redesign process, selecting 10 high-ranking corridors, including Tremont Avenue, University Avenue, Washington Bridge / 181 Street, and Fordham Road / 207 Street / University Heights Bridge within CB5. [bx_cb5_projects_dec032019#p004_c0003]

By 2021, Fordham Road materials still framed Bronx bus-priority work as downstream of the 2019 action plan: they referenced the April 2019 release, 16.3 miles of bus lanes installed in 2020, and eight additional Bronx corridors, including Fordham Road, identified for priority treatments. [fordham_rd_major_deegan_expwy_boston_rd_jun2021#p004_c0003]

Retrospective Status

Later watchdog and budget-office sources treat the plan as an important but incomplete benchmark. A 2025 report states that the Better Buses speed target was abandoned and that local bus speeds remained around 8 mph seven years later. [life_in_slow_lane_2025#p005_c0003] Another 2025 analysis describes the 2019 Better Buses Action Plan as one of the first comprehensive citywide bus-service initiatives, but says citywide bus speeds in December 2024 were largely consistent with January 2019, with average peak weekday speed across 316 routes at 7.6 mph compared with 7.8 mph in January 2019. [speeding_up_slowly_2025#p007_c0004] [speeding_up_slowly_2025#p007_c0010]

The later evidence is mixed by treatment area rather than a simple total failure story. One report says NYC DOT had installed TSP at over 2,000 intersections by 2023, meeting the Streets Plan mandate, but also notes that impact reporting was missing; the same source says all-door boarding had not started outside SBS routes. [behind_schedule_2025#p024_c0001] [behind_schedule_2025#p027_c0005]
