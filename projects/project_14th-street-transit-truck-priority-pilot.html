14th Street Transit & Truck Priority Pilot Project

project_14th-street-transit-truck-priority-pilot

The 14th Street Transit & Truck Priority pilot was NYCDOT's October 2019 bus/truck-priority redesign for the M14 A/D SBS corridor, framed around faster and more reliable bus service, Vision Zero safety goals, and preserving 14th Street as an important truck route. From 6 AM to 10 PM, through travel on the corridor was limited to buses, trucks, and emergency vehicles; other vehicles could make local access trips but had to make the next available right turn, with left turns generally restricted. The pilot also added pedestrian space, painted curb extensions, bus boarding platforms, benches, signage and signal adjustments, and fixed plus bus-mounted camera enforcement. [14th_street_winter2020_monitoring#p002_c0006] [14th_street_winter2020_monitoring#p002_c0007] [14th_street_winter2020_monitoring#p002_c0008] [14th_street_winter2020_monitoring#p004_c0006]

By the Winter 2020 monitoring period, the source reported materially faster M14 service: weekday westbound trips were 13.9% to 14.5% faster and eastbound trips were 25.5% to 29.5% faster versus January 2018 baselines. Average daily ridership was also higher than the listed pre-implementation counts: 29,568 weekday riders, up 14%, and 22,366 Saturday riders, up 35%. M14 A and M14 D customer journey time performance was reported at 86% and 87%, above the systemwide SBS and local/limited comparisons shown in the same table. [14th_street_winter2020_monitoring#p007_c0011] [14th_street_winter2020_monitoring#p007_c0013] [14th_street_winter2020_monitoring#p008_c0010] [14th_street_winter2020_monitoring#p008_c0017]

The monitoring reports also preserve the tradeoffs. The Winter 2020 report says most parallel east-west streets saw only minor travel-time increases, but flags likely project-related congestion around 13th Street near University Place. Public survey responses were strongly positive for bus riders, while open feedback still raised side-street congestion, the 5th Avenue stop, bus bunching and frequency, and pickup/drop-off confusion. Business feedback was mixed: many respondents reported customer counts, loading, or overall business conditions stayed the same, but notable shares, especially food/beverage, dry retail, and service respondents, reported decreases or worsened conditions. [14th_street_winter2020_monitoring#p010_c0005] [14th_street_winter2020_monitoring#p010_c0007] [14th_street_winter2020_monitoring#p017_c0004] [14th_street_winter2020_monitoring#p019_c0006] [14th_street_winter2020_monitoring#p020_c0013] [14th_street_winter2020_monitoring#p021_c0010] [14th_street_winter2020_monitoring#p021_c0012] [14th_street_winter2020_monitoring#p021_c0016]

A later NYC DOT page treats the pilot as the predecessor to the permanent 14th Street Busway, saying the busway was made permanent in June 2020 after the pilot. [14th_street_busway#p001_b0001]

Two caveats matter when reading the monitoring data. The Fall 2019 preliminary report deferred crash data to later reports, and the Winter 2020 report later stated a 42% decrease in injury crashes from October 2017-January 2018 to October 2019-January 2020; that is a monitoring-period comparison, not a standalone causal attribution. The Winter 2020 report also says its data was collected in January and February 2020 before the March 22, 2020 NY PAUSE order, and that future quarterly data would not be gathered until travel conditions returned to more typical activity levels.
