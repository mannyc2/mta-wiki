Overview

The LaGuardia Airport Access Alternatives Analysis began in May 2011 after NYCDOT received a December 2010 FTA grant to study transit access alternatives to LGA, and the June 2011 deck defined the focus as low-cost improvements within a short timeframe. [110622_lga_aa_slides#p004_c0003] [110622_lga_aa_slides#p004_c0004] [110622_lga_aa_slides#p004_c0005]

The October 2012 handout describes LGA as the only New York-area airport not served by rapid transit and says the study was a partnership among NYCDOT, MTA New York City Transit, MTA Bus Company, and Port Authority of New York and New Jersey. [2012_10_lga_plan_one_pager#p001_c0003]

The June 2011 deck placed the LaGuardia/East Elmhurst Corridor on the short list for the next Select Bus Service route. [110622_lga_aa_slides#p003_c0007] [110622_lga_aa_slides#p003_c0008]

Need And Goals

The 2011 slides listed existing LGA bus service as M60, Q33, Q47, Q48, and Q72, and they said bus service was often slowed by narrow streets and long dwell times. [110622_lga_aa_slides#p006_c0004] [110622_lga_aa_slides#p006_c0005]

The same slides said over two-thirds of LGA passengers came from NYC, most from Manhattan, and over 85% of LGA passengers used taxi or private car. [110622_lga_aa_slides#p007_c0003] [110622_lga_aa_slides#p008_c0003]

The 2012 handout stated the study goals as improving transit access to LGA, improving transit service in nearby neighborhoods, and providing low-cost transit improvements in a short timeframe. [2012_10_lga_plan_one_pager#p001_c0006]

The 2011 slides framed potential alignments as connecting LGA to nearby commercial hubs including Astoria, Harlem, The Hub, Midtown, East Elmhurst/Jackson Heights, and Flushing. [110622_lga_aa_slides#p023_c0003] [110622_lga_aa_slides#p023_c0004]

Alternatives And Mode Choice

The AA process was described as identifying all reasonable transit alternatives, selecting the alternative that best met goals and objectives, and qualifying the project for future FTA funding for environmental review, detailed planning, and design. [110622_lga_aa_slides#p005_c0004] [110622_lga_aa_slides#p005_c0005] [110622_lga_aa_slides#p005_c0006]

The June 2011 modal alternatives were Bus Rapid Transit, Light Rail Transit, Ferry, Automated Guideway Transit, Streetcar, and Heavy Rail. [110622_lga_aa_slides#p018_c0004] [110622_lga_aa_slides#p018_c0005] [110622_lga_aa_slides#p018_c0006] [110622_lga_aa_slides#p018_c0007] [110622_lga_aa_slides#p018_c0008] [110622_lga_aa_slides#p018_c0009]

By the November 2011 workshop summary, Bus Rapid Transit had been selected as the locally preferred mode because it fulfilled the study's goals for airport access, neighborhood transit, and cost-effective short-term options. [2012_04_20_brt_lga_workshop_summary#p001_c0013] [2012_04_20_brt_lga_workshop_summary#p001_c0014]

The October 2012 handout identified BRT as the mode best suited to improving LGA service and linked it to the Select Bus Service brand used on other NYC corridors. [2012_10_lga_plan_one_pager#p001_c0004]

Public Input

Public Meeting #2 was held on November 2, 2011 in workshop format with 23 participants across four small groups. [2012_04_20_brt_lga_workshop_summary#p001_c0005]

The workshop covered a project introduction, route ideas for connecting key points, rider preferences, and BRT features, and participants returned 18 surveys at the end. [2012_04_20_brt_lga_workshop_summary#p001_c0006] [2012_04_20_brt_lga_workshop_summary#p006_c0008]

Participant-identified route ideas included Bx41/Bx55 from the northern Bronx via the RFK Bridge, Q105 from Midtown via Northern Boulevard, Q53 from Rockaway Park, and Q48 or Q26 from North Shore Towers. [2012_04_20_brt_lga_workshop_summary#p003_c0006]

Preference voting recorded 16 votes for a 60-minute one-seat ride and 3 votes for a 45-minute one-transfer ride. [2012_04_20_brt_lga_workshop_summary#p005_c0006]

The same table recorded 16 votes for express bus, 15 for highway routing, 9 for luggage racks, and 16 for a 35-minute ride in a dedicated lane. [2012_04_20_brt_lga_workshop_summary#p005_c0006]

Survey results said participants preferred real-time bus information, enhanced stations, off-set bus lanes, and off-board fare collection, and did not prefer curbside bus lanes. [2012_04_20_brt_lga_workshop_summary#p006_c0009]

2012 SBS Direction

The October 2012 handout presented proposed new routes under Manhattan/Bronx and Queens groupings. [2012_10_lga_plan_one_pager#p001_c0007] [2012_10_lga_plan_one_pager#p001_c0008] [2012_10_lga_plan_one_pager#p001_c0017]

For Manhattan/Bronx, current service issues were long dwell times, long signal delays, and traffic congestion on 125th Street; for Queens, current service issues were long dwell times, long signal delays, and slow travel speeds on residential streets. [2012_10_lga_plan_one_pager#p001_c0009] [2012_10_lga_plan_one_pager#p001_c0010] [2012_10_lga_plan_one_pager#p001_c0018] [2012_10_lga_plan_one_pager#p001_c0019]

The handout listed M60 SBS as 20% faster to LGA, the proposed Bronx SBS route as 40% faster, and proposed Queens service as over 40% faster than current local service. [2012_10_lga_plan_one_pager#p001_c0012] [2012_10_lga_plan_one_pager#p001_c0013] [2012_10_lga_plan_one_pager#p001_c0021]

The listed Manhattan/Bronx features were limited stops, dedicated bus lanes, transit signal priority, off-board fare payment, and luggage racks. [2012_10_lga_plan_one_pager#p001_c0016]

The listed Queens features were limited stops, highway operation, and transit signal priority. [2012_10_lga_plan_one_pager#p001_c0024]

The handout also said nearly 10,000 daily M60 riders traveled entirely on 125th Street and that bus improvements would benefit more than 32,000 daily riders on the corridor through reduced delays and improved reliability. [2012_10_lga_plan_one_pager#p002_c0008]

As next steps, the handout listed addressing stakeholder comments, selecting locally preferred alignments, preparing a final AA report, starting detailed route analysis, and implementation in 2013/2014. [2012_10_lga_plan_one_pager#p002_c0016]
