S79 Select Bus Service Launch

S79 Select Bus Service began on September 2, 2012, with the S79 converted to SBS service stopping only at marked SBS stations while the S59 and S78 continued to serve local stops with added service. [2012_08_hylan_start_of_service_flyer#p001_c0001] [2012_08_hylan_start_of_service_flyer#p001_c0002] A later progress report describes the project as an MTA New York City Transit and NYC DOT implementation along the S79 route, primarily on Hylan Boulevard, intended to improve S79, local, and express bus service while also addressing driver and pedestrian conditions. [2014_hylan_blvd_final_report#p002_c0002]

The corridor context was unusually broad for a bus route project: Hylan Boulevard was described as a primary east/south shore Staten Island corridor, carrying more than 40,000 daily vehicles at New Dorp Lane and more than 32,000 daily bus customers across local and express routes. [2014_hylan_blvd_final_report#p003_c0002] [2014_hylan_blvd_final_report#p003_c0003] [2014_hylan_blvd_final_report#p003_c0004] The S79 itself was a 15-mile route from Staten Island Mall to the 86th Street subway station in Bay Ridge via Hylan Boulevard and Richmond Avenue, with more than 2.9 million annual passengers and nearly 9,000 customers on an average weekday. [2014_hylan_blvd_final_report#p003_c0005]

The main service changes were stop consolidation and a more direct path: the former S79 local made 80 stops in each direction, while S79 SBS reduced that to 18 stations, and three travel-path revisions near Staten Island Mall and Eltingville Transit Center were credited with saving about five to nine minutes in each direction. [2014_hylan_blvd_final_report#p007_c0007] [2014_hylan_blvd_final_report#p010_c0010] Street treatments included offset bus lanes on Richmond Avenue near Staten Island Mall and approaching Hylan Boulevard, curbside peak-period bus lanes on Hylan Boulevard between Lincoln Avenue and Steuben Street, coordinated traffic signals, new or repaired sidewalks, ADA ramps at 32 locations, and pedestrian safety islands at five signalized intersections. [2014_hylan_blvd_final_report#p010_c0004] [2014_hylan_blvd_final_report#p010_c0005] [2014_hylan_blvd_final_report#p007_c0004] [2014_hylan_blvd_final_report#p009_c0003] [2014_hylan_blvd_final_report#p009_c0005]

The reported results were strongest on travel time and ridership. Comparing summer 2012 to March 2013, average end-to-end S79 travel time fell from 71 to 62 minutes northbound to Brooklyn and from 68 to 55 minutes southbound to Staten Island Mall, described as 13% and 19% reductions. [2014_hylan_blvd_final_report#p014_c0005] The executive summary also reported a 13-19% S79 speed improvement, S79 ridership growth of 10.8%, combined S59/S78/S79 growth of 6.6%, a 1.2% increase on other Staten Island routes, no substantial traffic-flow impact, and pedestrian benefits including sidewalks and shelters. [2014_hylan_blvd_final_report#p002_c0003] Rider surveys conducted before and after implementation found favorable satisfaction, with 97% of S79 SBS riders satisfied or very satisfied; dissatisfaction was mainly tied to skipped closest stops and added transfers. [2014_hylan_blvd_final_report#p018_c0009] [2014_hylan_blvd_final_report#p019_c0004]

Transit Signal Priority was advertised in the launch flyer as "coming in 2013," but the 2014 report still described active TSP as expected in summer 2014, so this page treats TSP as planned rather than confirmed implemented by launch. [2012_08_hylan_start_of_service_flyer#p002_c0007] [2014_hylan_blvd_final_report#p020_c0004]
