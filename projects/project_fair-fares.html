Overview

Fair Fares is a City-funded fare-discount program that gives eligible New York City residents a 50% discount on subway, local bus, and SBS bus fares. [behind_schedule_2025#p031_c0001]

The program launched in 2019 for New Yorkers with incomes below the federal poverty level, expanded eligibility to 120% of FPL in 2023, and expanded again to 145% of FPL in 2025. [behind_schedule_2025#p031_c0001]

The 2025 eligibility expansion made another 200,000 people eligible, and Fair Fares funding was $115 million in the FY2025 budget, $20 million above the prior year. [behind_schedule_2025#p031_c0001]

As of February 2025, enrolled Fair Fares riders could use OMNY to pay for trips, but the Fair Fares discount did not apply to express buses. [behind_schedule_2025#p031_c0001]

Uptake And Gaps

The Comptroller report frames Fair Fares as important for affordable transit but says it remains less generous and reaches a smaller share of residents than similar programs in other U.S. cities. [behind_schedule_2025#p031_c0002]

By the end of 2024, about 360,000 New Yorkers were enrolled in Fair Fares, which the report says represented 38% of the eligible population. [behind_schedule_2025#p031_c0002]

The report attributes limited uptake partly to low awareness and more strongly to eligibility rules that exclude many regular commuters whose incomes are too high to qualify. [behind_schedule_2025#p031_c0002]

At the 145% FPL threshold, the report says a family of four making $47,000 would not be eligible, and it says many people who do qualify report infrequent public-transit use. [behind_schedule_2025#p031_c0002]

Outreach And Enrollment

MTA board minutes describe a March 30 Fair Fares day of action where Department of Social Services staff worked with NYCT station staff to tell customers about the program and enroll eligible customers on the spot. [meeting_doc_140461#p003_c0004]

An MTA fare-compliance presentation says the agency created 15 Customer Service Centers and integrated Fair Fares enrollment across all boroughs. [meeting_doc_140676#p004_c0004]

The same presentation says MTA expanded marketing campaigns for the Reduced-Fare and Fair Fares programs across subways and buses. [meeting_doc_140676#p004_c0005]

A later slide lists equipping 15 Customer Service Centers for Fair Fares enrollment with the City. [meeting_doc_140676#p011_c0004]

Policy Questions

The Comptroller report recommends expanding Fair Fares to New Yorkers with incomes up to 200% of FPL and to CUNY students in order to reach more regular commuters and transit users. [behind_schedule_2025#p009_c0006]

The report says advocates have called for a 200% FPL threshold and cites a Community Service Society estimate that such an expansion would cost about $150 million per year, $35 million above current spending levels. [behind_schedule_2025#p031_c0002]

The report separately estimates that making all CUNY students eligible would add 118,000 students at a cost of about $57 million per year, with enrollment designed to happen directly on campus using CUNY IDs. [behind_schedule_2025#p038_c0005]

The report also recommends expanding fare-discount programs to cover express buses at all times of day, noting that Fair Fares riders cannot apply the discount to express bus trips. [behind_schedule_2025#p038_c0006]
