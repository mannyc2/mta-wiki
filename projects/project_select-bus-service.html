Program Role

Select Bus Service is best read as a program umbrella for New York City's SBS/BRT corridor work, not as a single route or corridor. One program report dates SBS's inception to 2008 and credits SBS services with saving 3.5 million hours in passenger travel time while serving more than 20,000 additional daily bus passengers. [brt_routes_fullreport#p005_c0003] Bus Forward frames SBS as a response to declining ridership and slower bus service, with reported outcomes including 10-30% faster bus speeds, about 10% ridership growth, more reliable service, 95% customer satisfaction, and safer streets. [bus_forward_2#p003_c0003] [bus_forward_2#p003_c0004]

The 2017 Bus Forward planning frame ties SBS expansion to legislation and interagency planning: 2015 City Council legislation called for NYC DOT to develop an updated SBS implementation plan in partnership with the MTA, and the Citywide Transit Plan was intended to build on the 2009 BRT plan by expanding the SBS network while also identifying complementary transit projects beyond BRT. [bus_forward_2#p002_c0001] [bus_forward_2#p002_c0003]

Treatment Package

SBS is described as a bundle of corridor-specific treatments rather than a single intervention. The 2024 bus lane camera report says the program integrated camera-enforced bus lanes with other corridor-specific improvements, and it explicitly cautions that camera enforcement's effect is hard to isolate from simultaneous strategies such as off-board fare payment. [bus_lane_camera_report_2024#p005_c0002] Camera enforcement began as an SBS-specific authority: in summer 2010, New York City and the MTA received legislative authority for camera-based bus lane enforcement only on SBS corridors, and stationary bus-lane cameras began implementation in November 2010. [bus_lane_camera_report_2024#p002_c0003]

The enforcement system itself has multiple pieces. DOT's fixed camera arrays capture driving-in-the-bus-lane violations from street-mounted locations, while MTA's bus-mounted ABLE system captures no-standing and no-parking violations; the 2024 report says the two systems address different violation types and that continued rollout of both is important to bus-lane effectiveness. [bus_lane_camera_report_2024#p006_c0003] [bus_lane_camera_report_2024#p009_c0001]

Network Expansion

Bus Forward identified 21 candidate corridors for the next phase of SBS based on data analysis and public feedback, with candidates spanning the Bronx, Brooklyn, Manhattan, Queens, and Staten Island. [bus_forward_2#p004_c0002] [bus_forward_2#p005_c0001] Woodhaven/Cross Bay shows how the program translated into corridor planning: NYC DOT and MTA hosted a June 25, 2014 public workshop to gather design input for implementing SBS along that corridor, with 54 participants and discussion of street designs and bus stops. [2014_06_25_brt_woodhaven_publicworkshop2_summary#p002_c0001]

Results And Open Data Questions

Program-level performance figures vary by document date and scope. A later summary card cites 100 million SBS trips citywide, 8 million person-hours saved, and 38+ miles of SBS bus lanes. [brt_routes_fullreport#p011_c0005] [brt_routes_fullreport#p011_c0006] [brt_routes_fullreport#p011_c0007] The 2024 camera report gives route-dependent SBS speed increases of 15% to 31%, while a later operations note describes 20 SBS routes, fare-payment-machine upkeep and relocation needs, and travel speed gains of up to 15-20%. [bus_lane_camera_report_2024#p005_c0002] [meeting_doc_167246#p010_c0011] [meeting_doc_167246#p010_c0012]

The strongest caution is measurement scope. The 2024 camera report says newer reporting requirements for bus speeds, reliability, and ridership were not included for 2022-2023 because the data had not been collected for that purpose; it says the next two-year report would incorporate those measures. [bus_lane_camera_report_2024#p003_c0004] [bus_lane_camera_report_2024#p003_c0005]
