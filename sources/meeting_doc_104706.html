### [p006_c0001] 2. CHAIR LIEBER'S REMARKS.
[p006_c0002] 
Chair Lieber thanked everyone for joining this month's meeting—the Board's final gathering for the year.
[p006_c0003] 
Chair Lieber, recapping the year's events, stated that the MTA began the year with a total of 3.4 million riders across all the agencies, including TBTA, as well as transit and commuter railroads. Chair Lieber stated that service is at 51% on subways this year and 77% on commuter railroads, which is real progress. Chair Lieber stated that ridership is not the only area where the organization has made gains, and he noted that service delivery on subways has now approached 95%, up from barely 90% at the start of this year when the agency was struggling with staffing shortages brought on by the Omicron wave, and when the agency had to suspend six subway lines because there were not enough people to staff the trains.
[p006_c0004] 
Chair Lieber stated that today's statistics show that the MTA is in a totally different place with much better service, including on subways and buses. The Chair applauded Rich Davey, President of New York City Transit, Craig Cipriano, Chief Operating Officer, NYCT, Demetrius Crichlow, Senior Vice President, Subways, and Frank Annicaro, Acting President, MTA Bus Company for everything they have done to hire and train new employees in service-sensitive positions faster than ever before. Chair Lieber applauded President Catherine Rinaldi for leading both MTA railroads to historic highs for on-time performance—LIRR 96%, MNR 97%. Chair Lieber stated that in countless ways, the MTA's team is making positive changes and delivering results for New Yorkers.
[p006_c0005] 
Chair Lieber highlighted the successful completion of the Bronx local bus network redesign, and he stated that many of the Bronx bus routes had not been updated in generations and the changes have already yielded significant time savings for Bronx riders. Chair Lieber noted that redesigns of the bus system in Queens and Brooklyn are also underway, and the agency continues to work with the city to achieve Mayor Adams' ambitious goals for bus lanes and to expand Automated Bus Lane Enforcement. Chair Lieber stated that he would like the bus drivers who spoke today during the public speakers' session to know that the agency is working hard to make the bus drivers' jobs easier and safer on the road every day.

Chair Lieber stated that every improvement is important as the organization strives to deliver better service for its customers, many of whom do not have other commuting options and cannot afford a $50 Uber. Chair Lieber stated that New York does not have room or space on the roads for more cars, and he stated that not only is transit essential to the economy and to equity, but it is also vitally important as the antidote to climate change. The Chair stated that New York's population density, which is possible only because of its great mass transit system, makes the city the cleanest, greenest place to live in the country—it is counterintuitive, but New York is the model of green. The Chair presented an image of an interactive map published by the New York Times last week that highlighted the much lower carbon footprint that New Yorkers have compared to less dense, less transit rich areas, and the Chair stated that in order to maintain this lower carbon footprint, the MTA needs to be able to run service that is an attractive alternative to driving, and the agency cannot accomplish this goal unless the MTA is fully funded. Chair Lieber stated that mass transit is an essential service, like police, fire, and sanitation services and New Yorkers cannot live without mass transit.

Chair Lieber stated that the MTA does not have all the tools to fix its financial problem but is required to legally adopt a budget. The Chair stated that the plan does balance the budget, includes some reasonable assumptions, but it also preserves flexibility as to how to fully achieve a balanced budget, and the MTA needs to prepare nevertheless for the possibility that there will have to be fare and toll increases at some point. Chair Lieber stated that transportation was a huge centerpiece of the New New York report, that includes the following priorities:
- [p008_c0003] - Creating a sustainable MTA operating budget model. - Increasing subway and bus service frequency. - Continuing to expand the subway and commuter rail network to allow riders to have faster commutes and more convenient use of mass transit. - An endorsement of the congestion pricing plan to get cars off the road and to fund mass transit.

### [p024_c0005] Discussion:
[p024_c0006] 
This contract (the “Contract”) provides for flood mitigation at the Coney Island Yard Complex in Brooklyn. MTA Construction and Development (“C&D”) requests that the Board ratify a modification to the Contract to replace the functionality of 11 damaged signal cables utilizing the new Programmable Logic Controller (“PLC”) system for a total cost of $1,309,000. In addition, this modification will include an excusable delay of 104 calendar days, extending the substantial completion date of the Contract to April 14, 2023.
[p024_c0007] 
The Contract calls for the construction of a flood wall along the perimeter of the Coney Island Yard Complex (the “Complex”), which extends approximately 30 feet below grade and approximately 12-15 feet above grade. On February 17, 2022, while installing 40-foot steel sheet piles along Shore Road near Gate 4 of the Complex, the Contractor inadvertently severed a bundle of underground signal cables. There were no indications or signal monuments identifying the existence of signal cables in the vicinity nor were signal cables depicted in the as-built drawings provided to the contractor. A temporary repair was performed by NYCT’s Department of Subways - Signal Department (“DOS-Signals”).

Cited by 1 record: Coney Island Yard Complex Long Term Flood Mitigation

[p024_c0008] 
Rather than replace the damaged signal cables in kind, C&D and DOS-Signals determined that the functionality of the signal cables could be satisfied by using the existing new PLC system. PLCs are digital devices with programmable memory that can be used to store instructions for controlling train movement. Using the PLC system was found to be more reliable and cost efficient than replacing the damaged cables, which would have involved running eleven new 100 pair cables approximately 2000 LF from the Coney Island Yard Junction Box to the Stillwell Terminal Signal Relay Room. The work to be performed under this modification includes modifying the logic and circuit designs of the existing PLC system to assume the functionality of the 11 damaged signal cables, installing additional PLC equipment, including fiber optic cables, performing testing and tagging of wires, updating wire diagrams and submitting design drawings.
[p024_c0009] 
The Contractor submitted a proposal in the amount of $3,741,889. Negotiations were held and the parties agreed to a lump sum cost of $1,309,700 which is considered fair and reasonable.
[p024_c0010]

Enacted by the New York State legislation and included in the 2020-2021 Enacted Budget, PAL 1266 (12-a) created a valuation procedure for transfers to or acquisitions by the MTA of NYC owned real property, or interests in real property, needed by the MTA for certain capital projects in the 2015-2019 and 2020-2024 capital programs. PAL 1266 (12-a) requires valuations of NYC's real property interests and negotiations with NYC to determine fair market value to be conducted after 1) the MTA has identified the need for such property interests, and 2) NYC has consented to their transfer or acquisition. The legislation was enacted to enable the MTA to acquire from NYC the real property interests needed for critical MTA capital projects timely, efficiently, and cost-effectively.
[p033_c0007] 
Pursuant to PAL 1266 (12-a), after the MTA acquires title to the real property interests, the MTA will make a written offer to pay NYC the fair market value for the said real property interests, based on an appraisal, and if needed will commence negotiations with NYC to determine fair market value. MTA Real Estate will inform the MTA Board after negotiations with NYC have concluded and the fair market value for the said real property interests is determined.
