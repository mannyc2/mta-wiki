## Page 2

### [p002_c0001] Why redesign the bus network in Queens?
### [p002_c0002] Queens has more bus routes, and bus riders, than any other borough
[p002_c0003] 115 bus routes that serve 800,000 customers on an average weekday (pre-pandemic)
### [p002_c0004] Queens is growing and evolving
[p002_c0005] Jamaica, Flushing, and Long Island City are booming, as are employment centers located farther away from the subway
### [p002_c0006] But the Queens bus network has not substantially changed in decades
[p002_c0007] Many routes follow old trolley lines
[p002_c0008] Bus speeds continue to decline

## Page 5

### [p005_c0001] Four route types inform bus stop spacing and service frequency
[p005_c0002] Local Routes connect local neighborhoods
[p005_c0003] Limited Routes serve high demand corridors with frequent service
[p005_c0004] SBS or “Crosstown” Routes connect key destinations across longer distances
[p005_c0005] “Rush” Routes connect outer borough neighborhoods quickly to the subway
> [p005_c0006] A detailed map of New York City showing a complex network of bus routes and subway lines. The map uses a color-coded system: green for local routes, red for limited routes, blue for SBS or "Crosstown" routes, and purple for "Rush" routes. The map covers a large area of the city, including the Manhattan, Bronx, Brooklyn, and Queens boroughs. Key landmarks such as the Port Authority Bus Terminal, JFK International Airport, and the Hudson River are visible. The map illustrates how different route types are integrated into the city's public transportation system, with bus stops and service frequencies varying based on the route type.
