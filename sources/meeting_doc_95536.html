### [p007_c0002] 2. CHAIR LIEBER'S REMARKS.
[p007_c0003] 
Chair Lieber thanked the public speakers, stating that it is important that the MTA hear directly from its stakeholders and hear from and interact directly with the public. Chair Lieber stated that many of the public speakers are part of the MTA's stakeholder advisory groups in one way or another, and he noted that public interaction is one aspect of the MTA's work that is incredibly important. The Chair thanked the public speakers for taking the time to come out and participate.
[p007_c0004] 
Chair Lieber thanked everyone for joining today's meeting. Chair Lieber noted that there were substantive discussions at Monday's committee meetings, and the Chair thanked the Board, especially the new members, for their very substantive contributions as the organization prepares to face one of the MTA's biggest challenges ever, which is the looming fiscal cliff discussed at some length on Monday.
[p007_c0005] 
Chair Lieber stated that, although government at all levels is not always known for taking on the hard issues, but with Governor Hochul's support, the MTA is taking on challenging issues: congestion pricing, redesigning the antiquated bus routes, and dramatically accelerating progress toward ADA accessibility. MTA celebrated the ADA settlement and the commitment of the MTA to making the entire subway system, along with its fully accessible bus system, fully accessible, and the partnership with the disability community is an incredibly important part of the process. Chair Lieber stated that MTA is trying to figure out, with the leadership of people like Board Member David Jones, how to have a fair and just system that will help reduce fare evasion—a complicated issue. In addition, MTA is executing transformative projects that were talked about for generations but never delivered—East Side Access, Third Track, Metro-North Penn Access (changing the lives of hundreds of thousands of people in the East Bronx), the Second Avenue Subway (a project that was promised to East Harlem since the '40s when they started knocking down the elevated train), and soon, with Governor Hochul's leadership, the reconstruction of existing Penn Station, the largest transportation facility in North America.
[p007_c0006] 
Chair Lieber stated that the MTA is being transparent about all the issues it is facing, and the biggest issue is the financial tsunami, as referred to by former Chair Patrick Foye, brought on by COVID. The Chair stated that dealing with these issues is the only way forward if the MTA is going to be able to support the region's recovery and deliver on its mission, which is an equity mission as well as a mobility mission—that every community, especially those that do not have the option to get into a $50 Uber and get where they need to go, and true accessibility for all. Chair Lieber stated that he knows that every member of this Board takes those responsibilities incredibly seriously.
[p007_c0007] 
Chair Lieber stated that Monday's committee meetings were busy, especially as previously stated, the Finance committee meeting, where CFO Kevin Willens unveiled the July Financial Plan, which the Chair stated came as no surprise since there have been many discussions about

Chair Lieber stated that for well over a year, ridership was tracking to McKinsey's 2020 projections, and those projections had assumed that COVID would be a two-year pandemic that would recede after 2-3 distinct waves or surges thanks to the anticipated widespread vaccination. The Chair stated that a lot has changed since those initial projections, most important, the pandemic dragged out, with multiple COVID waves and a slow and steady shift in habits and attitudes that entrenched a certain level of remote work.
[p008_c0003] 
Chair Lieber stated that also unpredicted at the time of McKinsey's 2020 report was frankly the upsurge in subway crime and disorder, which has had a measurable impact on rider and lapsed rider attitudes and experience. As a result of these variables, ridership since Omicron has returned more slowly than expected—at just 60% of pre-COVID numbers, when prior estimates had us by the end of this year closing in on 80-85%. Chair Lieber stated that the bottom line is that the operating deficit in the July Plan is projected to be $500 million larger and it will come sooner than previously anticipated.

Chair Lieber stated that the MTA is not providing that much more service than in the bad days of the 1990s when the agency had the same level of ridership as it is having post-COVID.

Chair Lieber stated that the MTA is not sitting on its hands until the Legislature is back in session in January—part of doing the hard stuff is being innovative and looking for new sources of revenue, which is what the agency is doing with the Transit Wireless contract, as discussed by Deputy CDO – Planning Fredericka Cuenca at Monday's Finance Committee.

Chair Lieber stated that the MTA is also looking to provide financial support for key capital projects that have recently taken shape since the original 2020-2024 Capital Program was adopted—both reconstruction projects like Metro-North Penn Station Access, and the design phase of the Penn Reconstruction, but also for additional investments that have come up because of safety: subway platform doors, additional ADA upgrades, and a plan to more quickly and efficiently rebuild Metro-North’s indispensable Park Avenue Viaduct, which is one of the actions included in the Capital Program today.

Chair Lieber stated that the MTA is putting forth an amendment to the Capital Plan to make room for these projects while also adjusting to the reality of higher rates of inflation, as discussed in detail by Tim Mulligan, Senior Vice President, Operations, NYCT, on Monday, and which the Board will vote on today.
[p009_c0004] 
Chair Lieber stated that congestion pricing is obviously key to the MTA’s ability to deliver on all these projects, which represents $15 billion of the $55 billion Capital Plan, and the agency is quickly moving forward to get this historic program up and running. Chair Lieber stated that he leveled with the Board and with the public when the agency hit roadblocks in Washington, but with Governor Hochul’s support, the agency has overcome almost all those obstacles. Chair Lieber stated that he is happy to announce today that with the United States Department of Transportation’s (“USDOT”) approval of the Environmental Assessment—the environmental document that is the key to the federal process, expected very soon, the MTA is now expecting to publish the document for public comment early in August. The MTA will also schedule another six public hearings, on top of the 20 that have already taken place. Chair Lieber stated that Allison de Cerreño, who is the head of the MTA’s Central Business District Tolling program, will provide a presentation on congestion pricing later in the meeting.
[p009_c0005] 
Chair Lieber stated that this major progress on congestion pricing makes it the right time to empanel the Traffic Mobility Review Board (“TMRB”)—the group that was required by state legislation to be formed by the MTA Board and charged with analyzing all the issues: the pricing and the arguments for exemptions and discounts and figuring out a package that would be recommended to the Board for review and approval. Chair Lieber emphasized that forming the TMRB is an important step and a step in compliance with state law for the process of congestion pricing. The TMRB’s recommendations will come back to the MTA Board for adoption, rejection, or modification when the TMRB have completed their work.

Such procurements may be set aside for MWBEs and SDVOBs in order to promote participation of those certified firms in MTA contracts, as mandated by Article 15-A of the Executive Law and to assist MTA with meeting its M/WBE and SDVOB goals. As set-asides, such procurements are not required to be publicly advertised. Under the proposed contracts, individual work assignments will be awarded through a competitive mini-Request for Proposal ("RFP") process, among some or all firms in an amount not-to-exceed $1.5 million. The solicitation was also advertised in the Daily News, Engineering News-Record, Minority Commerce Weekly, New York State Contract Reporter, New York Construction Report, and on the C&D Website. ### [p044_c0005] Discussion:
[p044_c0006] 
NYC Transit requests MTA Board approval of a three-year extension to the contract for Ultrasonic Internal Rail Flaw Inspections. The contract was awarded pursuant to EO168 to DPR Ultrasonic Technologies, LLC (“DPR”) on November 1, 2017, and ratified by the December 2017 Board for a period of five years (through October 31, 2022), in the total estimated amount of $10,352,334, as a noncompetitive estimated quantity contract due to the specific ultrasonic testing performed to ensure track integrity, and the maintenance and technical support DPR provides on its rail testing equipment and technology. All services provided by DPR on this contract are performed by, and proprietary to, DPR. DPR is the only firm that can operate its equipment.
[p044_c0007] 
Under this contract, DPR performs ultrasonic testing of rail-using equipment of its own design mounted to NYC Transit-owned and operated Track Geometry Cars (“TGCs”). Ultrasonic inspection is a function of ensuring track integrity by detecting potential flaws in rail that may not be visible to the naked eye by emitting high-frequency soundwaves into the rail and subsequently measuring how those soundwaves move through the rail. By detecting and analyzing otherwise invisible flaws within the rail, the rail can be replaced before a failure occurs. The inspection process is continuous, enabling the detection of flaws without requiring the TGC vehicle to stop, minimizing its impact to revenue service operations. The data resulting from the inspection is then analyzed by DPR staff and presented to Department of Subways (“DOS”) personnel as Priority 1, Priority 2, or lesser rail defects to assist DOS in track maintenance and specifically rail replacement to ensure the track is safe for the operation of subway trains. This contract also includes maintenance and technical support of DPR’s equipment on the TGCs.
[p044_c0008] 
Services under this contract include the provision of on-vehicle staff to operate the ultrasonic testing equipment, independent analysis staff embedded with NYC Transit staff to mutually interpret the data collected, as well as pricing for maintenance and as-needed repairs of the equipment. Pricing on the base contract was negotiated as firm, fixed pricing for each year of its five-year term. DPR originally proposed $8,604,427 for this extension. Through discussions, DPR offered a unilateral price concession resulting in a final estimated expenditure amount of $7,849,087 over the extension period.
