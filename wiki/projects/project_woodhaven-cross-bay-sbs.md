Woodhaven/Cross Bay was framed as both a Q52/Q53 Select Bus Service project and a
complete-street redesign. By late 2015, DOT/MTA materials described the corridor as carrying more
than 30,000 daily bus riders, with one-way bus trips varying from 55 to 85 minutes, difficult
pedestrian crossings, high speeds, and congestion at bottlenecks. The stated goal was faster and
more reliable bus service, safer streets for all users, and improved traffic and local conditions.
[2015_12_01_brt_woodhaven_cb5#p001_c0003]

The implementation strategy deliberately split near-term service and street changes from a later
capital project. The 2017 operational project was to launch Q52/Q53 SBS with off-board fare
payment, street resurfacing, roadway safety work, key left-turn restrictions, transit signal
priority, bus lanes, pedestrian improvements, and median bus stops along 1.3 miles where service
roads already existed. The longer-term DDC capital project was described as adding more durable
transit and Vision Zero elements, including new or extended medians, streetscaping, Cross Bay bus
bulbs, and additional Woodhaven median bus stops. [2015_12_01_brt_woodhaven_cb5#p002_c0002]
[2015_12_01_brt_woodhaven_cb5#p002_c0004] [2015_12_01_brt_woodhaven_cb5#p002_c0006]

By January 2017, draft designs were complete and in detailed design, with 27 SBS station pairs
planned, all with fare payment machines, plus targeted bus-priority improvements north of Queens
Boulevard and south of 165th Avenue. The same update said the project was on schedule for a Fall
2017 SBS start, followed by adjustment, monitoring, evaluation, and a capital-project phase.
[brt_woodhaven_cb14_jan2017#p012_c0002] [brt_woodhaven_cb14_jan2017#p020_c0003]
[brt_woodhaven_cb14_jan2017#p021_c0002]

The Fall 2018 progress report records that Q52/Q53 SBS began service on November 12, 2017 from
Woodside to the Rockaways, primarily along Woodhaven and Cross Bay Boulevards. Reported early
outcomes included a 5-8% increase in average weekday Q52/Q53 ridership, reduced bunching and
improved schedule adherence, 82-87% of surveyed riders preferring SBS to the prior limited service,
and 9-10% travel-time improvements for the measured peak-direction corridor trips. The report also
kept the project in an ongoing safety/capital frame, listing public-art fencing, two phases of TSP,
a 2018-2019 street improvement project, and a multi-phase capital project beginning in 2020 as next
implementation steps. [brt_woodhaven_after_fall2018#p002_c0002]
[brt_woodhaven_after_fall2018#p003_c0002] [brt_woodhaven_after_fall2018#p017_c0002]
[brt_woodhaven_after_fall2018#p018_c0008] [brt_woodhaven_after_fall2018#p019_c0002]
[brt_woodhaven_after_fall2018#p020_c0005] [brt_woodhaven_after_fall2018#p029_c0002]

The same progress report is careful about uneven impacts: it says bus speeds improved in most
segments, but buses slowed north of Metropolitan Avenue while general traffic got faster, and
vehicle travel times changed little end to end with some slower and some faster segments. Safety
evidence is also mixed rather than final: corridor crashes resulting in injuries were reported down
6%, while the report notes two 2018 pedestrian deaths at Jamaica Avenue and says DOT remained
committed to further pedestrian and vehicular safety investments.
[brt_woodhaven_after_fall2018#p003_c0002] [brt_woodhaven_after_fall2018#p027_c0002]
