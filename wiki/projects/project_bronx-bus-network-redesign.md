## Project Arc

The Bronx Bus Network Redesign was New York City Transit's first borough-wide bus network redesign under the Fast Forward Plan's Reimagine the Bus Network initiative. The final plan says MTA analyzed bus service data and Bronx demographics, engaged elected officials, community boards, and communities in the Bronx and parts of Manhattan served by Bronx routes, then drew on the Existing Conditions and Draft Plan reports. Its own process timeline lists the Existing Conditions Report in February 2019, the Draft Plan in May 2019, and the Final Plan in October 2019 [bronx_bus_network_final_plan_2019#p004_c0002] [bronx_bus_network_final_plan_2019#p004_c0004].

The plan framed the redesign around faster and more reliable service, using increased frequency, more direct routings, bus stop balancing, enhanced connectivity, and expanded bus priority. It also treated implementation as an iterative step rather than an endpoint, saying service would continue to be refined based on ridership, performance, and customer feedback [bronx_bus_network_final_plan_2019#p005_c0004] [bronx_bus_network_final_plan_2019#p005_c0005] [bronx_bus_network_final_plan_2019#p004_c0003].

## Addendum And Scope

The 2021 addendum says MTA held a February 2020 public hearing on the proposed final plan, but paused the redesign for about 18 months in March 2020 because of the COVID-19 pandemic. The same addendum says the restart was announced in August 2021, that the redesign focused on Local, Limited, and Select Bus Service routes rather than express routes, and that implementation was planned for the following summer [bronx_bus_network_final_plan_addendum_2021#p002_c0003].

The addendum preserved most of the October 2019 stop changes, while noting a small number of updates to accommodate NYC DOT bus-priority projects and community needs. It also delayed the Bx6 SBS implementation, associated Bx5 and Bx6 Local schedule changes, and all-door boarding until 2023 to align with OMNY deployment and avoid installing temporary off-board fare equipment for only one year of use [bronx_bus_network_final_plan_addendum_2021#p003_c0002] [bronx_bus_network_final_plan_addendum_2021#p003_c0004].

## Service Design

In the addendum's strategy language, the redesign proposed 14 route alignment changes and two new routes, extended four routes, made three key subway-station connections, and improved frequency on nine routes along 11 corridors. Its stop-balancing strategy proposed removing about 400 bus stops, explaining that each removed stop saves an average of 20 seconds, sometimes a minute or more depending on location and time of day [bronx_bus_network_final_plan_addendum_2021#p004_c0003] [bronx_bus_network_final_plan_addendum_2021#p004_c0005] [bronx_bus_network_final_plan_addendum_2021#p004_c0007] [bronx_bus_network_final_plan_addendum_2021#p004_c0009].

The final plan's connectivity argument was that higher frequency and redesigned routing would improve access within the Bronx and to other boroughs, while making transfers easier and reducing wait times. It acknowledged that some riders would need an additional transfer and stated a policy that no rider should pay an additional transfer fare as a result of the Bronx Bus Network Redesign [bronx_bus_network_final_plan_2019#p012_c0002] [bronx_bus_network_final_plan_2019#p012_c0003] [bronx_bus_network_final_plan_2019#p012_c0006].

## Implementation And Status

MTA's 2021 annual report says the MTA Board approved the Bronx Local Bus Network Redesign final plan in December 2021 and expected borough-wide implementation in summer 2022. A January 2023 MTA document says the Bronx Local Bus Network Redesign Plan launched in June 2022 and showed a 7 percent AM speed increase; another 2023 MTA document described improvements in speed, frequency, and connections after the prior June's implementation [annual_report_narrative_2021#p027_c0002] [meeting_doc_104791#p028_c0010] [meeting_doc_111811#p006_c0005].

By June 2023, MTA materials described the redesign as reaching its first anniversary and as part of the Faster, Cleaner, Safer plan. That anniversary summary says the implemented redesign brought more direct routes, better connections, more all-day service, balanced bus stop spacing, two new routes, modifications to 13 routes, increased frequency on eight key corridors, and adjustments to closely spaced stops [meeting_doc_113986#p006_c0009] [meeting_doc_115246#p003_c0010].

## Bus Priority Links

Bus priority was explicitly tied to the redesign, but it was implemented through NYC DOT street projects rather than route planning alone. The addendum says NYC DOT analyzed 46 Bronx and Manhattan corridors and selected 10 high-ranking corridors for treatments such as new bus lanes, transit signal priority, bus boarders, and curb management, including future-plan, present-implementation, and completed-2020 corridors [bronx_bus_network_final_plan_addendum_2021#p004_c0012] [bronx_bus_network_final_plan_addendum_2021#p005_c0002] [bronx_bus_network_final_plan_addendum_2021#p005_c0003].

Soundview is the clearest source-backed link between the redesign and a specific bus-priority project. A December 2021 NYC DOT press release says DOT completed three miles of new bus-priority corridors on Story Avenue, Bronx River Avenue, and White Plains Road, serving 45,000 daily riders with bus lanes, pedestrian safety changes, and transit signal priority. The same release says DOT worked with MTA on the planned Bronx Bus Network Redesign and that the redesign had identified Soundview corridors serving Bx5, Bx36, and Bx39 as especially congested, slow, and unreliable [soundview_bus_priority_press_release_2021#p001_b0001].

## Caveats

The addendum's proposed package says 14 route alignment changes, nine routes with frequency improvements, and about 400 stop removals; MTA's June 2023 anniversary language says the implemented package had 13 route modifications, frequency increases on eight corridors, and stop-spacing adjustments. The repo evidence supports a strong relationship between the redesign and bus-priority corridor selection, but individual bus-priority projects remain separately materialized records. Some post-implementation performance claims are from MTA meeting materials, while later watchdog sources report different aggregate figures; this page keeps those evidence streams separate.
