## Overview

The Revised Key Station Plan is the subway-station accessibility plan that NYCT's annual ADA compliance reports treat as the core Key Station Plan implementation track. The reported legal origin is a 1994 amendment to New York Public Buildings Law and Transportation Law that expanded NYCT's target from 54 stations accessible by 2010 to 100 stations accessible by 2020; the revised plan initially specified 91 stations, leaving 9 to be identified later with the New York City Transportation Disabled Committee. [meeting_doc_105911#p002_c0004]

The plan was refined through FTA and disability-community review. The reports say 66 Street/Broadway and Prospect Park/Brighton were added as the 92nd and 93rd key stations, that three proposed changes were presented for public comment at a March 24, 2000 ADA Compliance Coordination Committee meeting, and that the proposals and five-year-period completion schedule were approved by FTA in June 2000. [meeting_doc_164781#p002_c0004] Later additions filled out the final sequence, including South Ferry as the 98th key station, Brooklyn Museum as the 99th, and Bedford Park Boulevard as the accepted 100th key station. [meeting_doc_164781#p002_c0005]

## Progress Across Reports

The December 2022 ADA report said 99 key stations in the Revised Key Station Plan were accessible and listed a single remaining key station under construction: 68 Street on the 6 line in Manhattan, with projected place-in-service and substantial completion dates of December 2024. [meeting_doc_105911#p003_c0005] [meeting_doc_105911#p005_c0002] [meeting_doc_105911#p005_c0004] The December 2023 report repeated the same 99-accessible-station count and listed 68 Street as the final key station still in construction with the same December 2024 projected dates. [meeting_doc_133246#p002_c0008] [meeting_doc_133246#p005_c0002] [meeting_doc_133246#p005_c0003]

By the December 2024 report, the key-station list had changed from 99 to 100 accessible key stations. The completed list includes 68 Street-Hunter College on the 6 line in Manhattan as item 100, so the source sequence moves the plan from one remaining construction item in 2022 and 2023 to a completed 100-station key-station list in 2024. [meeting_doc_164781#p002_c0008] [meeting_doc_164781#p004_c0001]

## Beyond The Key Station Plan

The reports separate the 100-station Key Station Plan from broader accessibility expansion. In 2022, NYCT reported 41 accessible non-key stations going beyond the Revised Key Station Plan; that count increased to 48 in the 2023 report and 56 in the 2024 report. [meeting_doc_105911#p005_c0006] [meeting_doc_133246#p005_c0005] [meeting_doc_164781#p005_c0002]

Fast Forward is presented as the next coverage program rather than as part of the original 100-key-station obligation: NYCT committed in 2018 to 50 additional accessible stations, increased the commitment to 70 in 2019, and stated the goal that no subway rider be more than two stops from an accessible station. [meeting_doc_105911#p002_c0006] [meeting_doc_164781#p006_c0002] For that later station-prioritization work, NYCT described consultation with the disability community, community boards, and elected officials, plus a February 6, 2019 public event, using criteria such as reducing gaps between accessible stations, improving transfer and terminal connectivity, proximity to activity centers, ridership, demographics, and cost or constructability. [meeting_doc_105911#p003_c0001]

The June 2022 class-action settlement is another broader accessibility commitment alongside the plan history. The 2022 report says the settlement affirmed MTA's accessibility commitment and required elevators or ramps to create a stair-free path of travel at 95 percent of then-inaccessible subway stations by 2055; later reports describe the commitment as having construction contracts in place by 2055. [meeting_doc_105911#p003_c0002] [meeting_doc_164781#p006_c0004]
