## Summary

The Climate Resilience Roadmap is MTA's framework for turning a climate vulnerability assessment into action. The January 2024 board briefing defines it as an assessment of current and future vulnerabilities, ten resilience strategies, and an implementation framework spanning capital projects, design practices, operating actions, and interagency actions. [meeting_doc_131986#p005_c0003] The briefing frames those actions around hazards projected between then and the 2050s: severe coastal floods becoming three times more likely, average monthly high tides rising up to 30 inches, torrential rainfall events doubling, and days above 90 degrees tripling, with the projections attributed to the New York City Panel on Climate Change's 2019 work. [meeting_doc_131986#p004_c0002] [meeting_doc_131986#p004_c0005] [meeting_doc_131986#p004_c0008] [meeting_doc_131986#p004_c0011] [meeting_doc_131986#p004_c0014] [meeting_doc_131986#p004_c0015]

## Vulnerability Picture

The source presents the roadmap as systemwide. It identifies 63% of the subway system as underground and vulnerable to torrential rain above the cited sewer-capacity threshold, seven subway yards as currently vulnerable to coastal surge and stormwater floods, and bus-depot exposure that includes 14 depots with unmitigated coastal-surge exposure and up to 11 depots currently impacted by torrential rain. [meeting_doc_131986#p007_c0004] [meeting_doc_131986#p008_c0004] [meeting_doc_131986#p009_c0004] It also ties bus operations to street flooding, stating that all tunnel and most bridge approaches are vulnerable during torrential rain and that 95% of bus routes traverse at least one street exposed to stormwater floods. [meeting_doc_131986#p010_c0004]

For rail infrastructure, the briefing points to track, yard, slope, and shoreline exposure. It says torrential rain affects regional railroad tracks and yards, with about 41% of Metro-North track and 19% of LIRR track adjacent to steep slopes or embankments, and projects monthly tidal-flood exposure for 64 miles of regional railroad track by the 2050s and 175 miles by the 2080s. [meeting_doc_131986#p012_c0004] [meeting_doc_131986#p013_c0004] Heat is also part of the roadmap's risk frame: station platforms and sensitive equipment are described as vulnerable to prolonged extreme heat, while outdoor track and equipment are vulnerable to expansion, compromised performance, and reduced useful life. [meeting_doc_131986#p011_c0004] [meeting_doc_131986#p011_c0006]

## Strategy And Implementation

The roadmap lists ten resilience strategies: shield subway stations and tunnels; protect subway yards; address open subway infrastructure; safeguard bus depots; manage floods on city streets; reduce LIRR flooding; reduce Metro-North flooding; expand underground ventilation; protect outdoor infrastructure from heat; and address heat and wind impacts on bridges. [meeting_doc_131986#p016_c0002] [meeting_doc_131986#p016_c0004] [meeting_doc_131986#p016_c0005] [meeting_doc_131986#p016_c0006] [meeting_doc_131986#p016_c0007] [meeting_doc_131986#p016_c0008] [meeting_doc_131986#p016_c0009] [meeting_doc_131986#p016_c0010] [meeting_doc_131986#p016_c0011] [meeting_doc_131986#p016_c0012] [meeting_doc_131986#p016_c0013] The implementation framework is explicitly multi-channel: capital projects, design practices, operating actions, and interagency actions. [meeting_doc_131986#p017_c0003] [meeting_doc_131986#p017_c0004] [meeting_doc_131986#p017_c0006] [meeting_doc_131986#p017_c0008] [meeting_doc_131986#p017_c0010]

The clearest worked example is Strategy 1, "Shield subway stations and tunnels," whose stated purpose is to keep stormwater out and remove stormwater that enters. [meeting_doc_131986#p018_c0002] [meeting_doc_131986#p018_c0003] The same case study reports installed vent and stair protections at 28 stations after the 2007 torrential rain, protections underway at 74 stations, DEP cleaning of 16 catch basins before every torrential rain, and cleaning of 9 siphons plus 13,500 linear feet of sewers adjacent to subways in 2022/23. [meeting_doc_131986#p019_c0005] [meeting_doc_131986#p019_c0006] [meeting_doc_131986#p019_c0007] [meeting_doc_131986#p019_c0008] [meeting_doc_131986#p019_c0010] [meeting_doc_131986#p019_c0011] The briefing then says 88 stations flooded in one or more torrential-rain events in 2023 and that MTA was advancing new capital projects, proactive operating actions, and ongoing interagency actions for those locations. [meeting_doc_131986#p020_c0004] [meeting_doc_131986#p020_c0005] [meeting_doc_131986#p020_c0006]

The Metro-North-focused case study is Strategy 7, "Reduce Metro-North flooding," scoped to the Hudson Line, Mott Haven Yard, Melrose/Tremont Corridor, and Harlem Line. [meeting_doc_131986#p021_c0001] Later April 2024 sources connect the roadmap to implementation scale and near-term railroad examples: Metro-North's president described the roadmap as a new MTA initiative requiring a $6 billion investment over the next decade, including upgrades to train yards and signal systems, to improve recovery from extreme weather and maintain service standards. [meeting_doc_138196#p006_c0007] In the April 29 committee minutes, she also cited a Mott Haven event, a $20 million State-budget initial investment in Hudson Line resiliency, and the Harlem Line signal-box elevation at MP 25.7 near Cleveland Street and Lakeview Avenue as current resilience actions. [meeting_doc_140311#p004_c0005] [meeting_doc_140311#p004_c0006]

## Timeline

The board briefing places the roadmap between the 2025-2044 Twenty Year Needs Assessment and the next five-year capital plan. It says the roadmap would outline proactive adaptation actions in anticipation of the five-year capital plan and beyond, with a timeline of the Twenty Year Needs Assessment in October 2023, the Climate Resilience Roadmap in March 2024, study/design launches for key projects, and the next five-year capital plan in fall 2024.
