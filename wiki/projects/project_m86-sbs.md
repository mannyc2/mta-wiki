The M86 SBS conversion is documented as a short crosstown Select Bus Service project for 86th
Street, connecting the Upper West Side and Upper East Side through Central Park. The 2017 progress
report frames the route as a strong SBS candidate because it had very high per-mile ridership but
declining ridership, travel times, and reliability before implementation. The same report says the
project used targeted intersection treatments, off-board fare payment, customer amenities, and
safety upgrades rather than a full corridor rebuild. [m86_sbs_progress_report_2017#p002_c0002]
[m86_sbs_progress_report_2017#p002_c0003]

Service launched on Monday, July 13, 2015 after several weeks of station construction and road
marking work. DOT and MTA NYCT used customer ambassadors during the launch period to explain
off-board fare payment and all-door boarding, then redeployed ambassadors in September for the
return-to-school period. [m86_sbs_progress_report_2017#p006_c0005]
[m86_sbs_progress_report_2017#p006_c0006]

The project combined several small but concrete bus-priority elements. Queue jump bus lanes were
installed at approaches to Central Park and Fifth Avenue, including westbound E 86th Street at 5th
Avenue and eastbound movements entering and leaving the Transverse Road. Off-board fare payment let
customers pay before boarding and use any of the three doors, with exceptions at a few drop-off or
special-case stops. The project also added real-time information and wayfinding totems at multiple
SBS stations. [m86_sbs_progress_report_2017#p004_c0010]
[m86_sbs_progress_report_2017#p005_c0004] [m86_sbs_progress_report_2017#p005_c0005]
[m86_sbs_progress_report_2017#p005_c0007] [m86_sbs_progress_report_2017#p005_c0009]

The progress report presents the post-launch results as broadly positive: ridership grew by an
average of 7% in the first 14 months of SBS service, travel times were 8-11% lower, reliability
improved by 10%, and surveyed overall customer satisfaction reached 96%. It also qualifies the
ridership result: fare-machine outages at some major stops affected measured ridership in several
months, and the new Second Avenue Subway service was expected to affect future ridership patterns.
[m86_sbs_progress_report_2017#p002_c0004] [m86_sbs_progress_report_2017#p002_c0005]
[m86_sbs_progress_report_2017#p006_c0010] [m86_sbs_progress_report_2017#p007_c0002]

The report separates the effect of queue jumps from the full SBS launch. Before off-board fare
payment began, queue jumps reduced time stopped in traffic by 7% westbound and 30% eastbound. Under
the full SBS build-out, eastbound travel time fell from 23.7 to 21.1 minutes, while westbound travel
time fell from 22.0 to 20.3 minutes. [m86_sbs_progress_report_2017#p007_c0020]
[m86_sbs_progress_report_2017#p007_c0021] [m86_sbs_progress_report_2017#p008_c0005]
[m86_sbs_progress_report_2017#p008_c0008] [m86_sbs_progress_report_2017#p008_c0010]
[m86_sbs_progress_report_2017#p008_c0013]

The remaining capital work in the 2017 report centered on pedestrian improvements: six neckdowns
and four bus bulbs along the M86 SBS route, including bus bulbs in both directions at Lexington and
3rd Avenues. Construction was described as delayed but underway in June 2016, with completion
anticipated in summer 2017. [m86_sbs_progress_report_2017#p011_c0004]
[m86_sbs_progress_report_2017#p011_c0005] [m86_sbs_progress_report_2017#p011_c0007]
[m86_sbs_progress_report_2017#p011_c0008]
