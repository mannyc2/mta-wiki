## Scope

The Madison Avenue Bus Improvement Project is a NYC DOT and MTA bus-priority proposal for Madison Avenue from East 23rd Street to East 42nd Street in Manhattan. The same project deck was presented to Manhattan Community Board 5 on May 19, 2025 and Manhattan Community Board 6 on June 2, 2025. [madison_ave_e23_st_e42_st_cb5_may2025#p001_c0002] [madison_ave_e23_st_e42_st_cb6_jun2025#p001_c0002]

The proposal extends the existing Madison Avenue northbound double bus-lane pattern south of East 42nd Street. The project sources state that bus lanes have existed on Madison Avenue from East 42nd Street to East 60th Street since 1981, and that the new proposal covers Madison Avenue from 23rd to 42nd Street, paired with existing southbound double bus lanes on Fifth Avenue from 60th to 34th Street. [madison_ave_e23_st_e42_st_cb6_jun2025#p004_c0002] [madison_ave_e23_st_e42_st_cb6_jun2025#p006_c0002]

## Corridor Need

The study area serves 92,000 daily bus riders on 34 routes: the M1, M2, M3, M4, and Q32 local buses plus 29 express bus routes from other boroughs. The CB6 deck also says more than 1,000 buses from those 34 routes serve the study area on weekdays. [madison_ave_e23_st_e42_st_cb6_jun2025#p004_c0002] [madison_ave_e23_st_e42_st_cb6_jun2025#p009_c0002]

The same source frames Madison Avenue as a high-ridership, low-speed corridor: buses carry 55% of roadway users during AM and PM rush hours while using less than a quarter of road space, and bus speeds are reported as low as 4.5 mph, compared with a Manhattan average of 6.2 mph and a citywide average of 8.1 mph. [madison_ave_e23_st_e42_st_cb6_jun2025#p004_c0002] Madison Avenue was also identified as a Bus Priority Corridor in the 2023 Streets Plan Update and as touching Tier 2 Priority Investment Areas. [madison_ave_e23_st_e42_st_cb6_jun2025#p005_c0004]

## Treatment Package

The core street design is a 24/7 double bus-lane extension from East 42nd Street to East 23rd Street. The proposal maintains west-curb parking and loading, keeps one general travel lane on most blocks, provides two general travel lanes at select blocks and hours, and maintains existing right turns. [madison_ave_e23_st_e42_st_cb6_jun2025#p016_c0001] [madison_ave_e23_st_e42_st_cb6_jun2025#p016_c0002]

The proposed cross-section shows a 12-foot curbside parking/rush-hour travel lane, one 11-foot travel lane, and two 11-foot bus-only lanes, for a 45-foot roadway section between the sidewalks. [madison_ave_e23_st_e42_st_cb6_jun2025#p016_c0003] [madison_ave_e23_st_e42_st_cb6_jun2025#p016_c0004]

Curb-management changes include maintaining No Standing on the east curb to allow the double bus lanes, adding half-block left-turn bays on the west curb at 27th, 29th, 31st, and 33rd Streets, adding rush-hour No Standing on the west curb between 36th and 37th Streets, and updating meter timing and other curb regulations on Madison Avenue and side streets. The plan says it would retain about 78% of existing parking spaces. [madison_ave_e23_st_e42_st_cb6_jun2025#p018_c0001] [madison_ave_e23_st_e42_st_cb6_jun2025#p018_c0002]

Automated Camera Enforcement is part of the operating concept. The deck says ACE enforcement covers vehicles driving in a bus lane, double parking, or standing at a bus stop; M2 and M4 Local buses were undergoing ACE expansion, and a later update would include future Madison Avenue bus lanes. [madison_ave_e23_st_e42_st_cb6_jun2025#p019_c0001] [madison_ave_e23_st_e42_st_cb6_jun2025#p019_c0002]

## Routes And Operations

The source route set ties the project to the Madison Avenue local routes M1, M2, M3, M4, and Q32, plus express service from other boroughs. The project map labels northbound service beginning on Madison Avenue for M4, BxM3/BxM4/BxM11, BxM6/BxM7/BxM8/BxM9/BxM10, M1/M2/M3, BM5, and other express groupings; it also labels southbound or Madison Square Park-bound service including Q32, SIM23/SIM24, SIM4C/SIM33C, X27/X37, X28/X38, BxM18, X68, SIM6/SIM11/SIM31, BM1/BM2/BM3/BM4, QM21, X63, X64, and X68. [madison_ave_e23_st_e42_st_cb6_jun2025#p004_c0002] [madison_ave_e23_st_e42_st_cb6_jun2025#p009_c0003]

October 2024 speed tables show local and express speeds at 42nd, 34th, and 28th Streets across AM peak, midday, and PM peak periods. The local entries range from 4.5 mph to 5.8 mph, while the express entries range from 5.2 mph to 8.7 mph; the deck notes that the speed analysis uses M1, M2, M3, BxM6, BxM7, BxM8, BxM9, and BxM10 data. [madison_ave_e23_st_e42_st_cb6_jun2025#p010_c0002] [madison_ave_e23_st_e42_st_cb6_jun2025#p010_c0003] [madison_ave_e23_st_e42_st_cb6_jun2025#p010_c0004] [madison_ave_e23_st_e42_st_cb6_jun2025#p010_c0005]

## Timeline And Status

The project was in design and planning during the 2025 community-board presentations. The next-steps slide lists spring 2025 for design refinement and traffic analysis, summer 2025 for planned implementation, and fall-winter 2025 for ongoing performance monitoring. [madison_ave_e23_st_e42_st_cb6_jun2025#p022_c0002]

The Better Buses project page also lists Madison Avenue as a current Better Buses project and describes the package as extending the existing double bus lanes south to East 23rd Street, adding left-turn bays at select cross streets, and changing curb regulations to improve speed and reliability for the 92,000 daily riders on 34 routes. [better_buses#p001_b0001]

## Outcomes Context

The project sources do not provide a Madison Avenue post-implementation outcome measurement. They do, however, cite the paired Fifth Avenue double bus lanes as precedent: after Fifth Avenue double bus lanes were implemented in 2018, 2017-to-2019 data showed local route speeds increasing 6-12% and express route speeds increasing 11-20%. [madison_ave_e23_st_e42_st_cb6_jun2025#p008_c0002]
