## Overview

Queens Boulevard Line East CBTC is the eastern Queens Boulevard/Hillside Avenue signal-modernization project, replacing fixed-block relay signaling from north of Union Turnpike to 179 St Station on the F line with CBTC, SSI, ATS, ACS, and an auxiliary wayside layer for degraded-mode protection. The scope modernizes four interlockings: 179 St, 169 St, Parsons Boulevard at Hillside Avenue, and Briarwood. [meeting_doc_179731#p042_c0003] [meeting_doc_179731#p042_c0004]

Delivery is split across E-J Electric, Mitsubishi, Siemens, and Hitachi roles. E-J Electric is responsible for AWS design, furnishing, and installation and deploys the Siemens-supplied DCS; Mitsubishi serves as system integrator and CBTC wayside supplier, reconfiguring ATS for QBL East with Siemens expansion tools; Hitachi supplies AWS equipment. Earlier materials identify this as Mitsubishi's first major CBTC project with MTA. [meeting_doc_179731#p042_c0004] [meeting_doc_135341#p044_c0004]

The project is also an interoperability step. MTA described it as expanding the QBL West interoperability demonstration from Siemens and Thales to a third CBTC supplier, Mitsubishi, with the existing Siemens QBL West system and new Mitsubishi QBL East system interchanging at Union Turnpike Interlocking. [meeting_doc_179731#p015_c0008] [meeting_doc_179731#p015_c0009]

## Timeline And Status

In November 2022, the project status table forecast Q2 2026 substantial completion, a $542.6 million budget, E-J Electric 10 percent complete, Mitsubishi 15 percent complete, and the Siemens DCS award still in progress. [meeting_doc_100281#p015_c0003] The companion IEC schedule note said the CBTC furnish and installer contracts had been awarded in December 2021, but the DCS task order was not yet awarded, the integrated project schedule was not being produced, and DCS/Hitachi delays could negatively affect the schedule. [meeting_doc_100281#p039_c0003]

By June 2023, the DCS task order had been awarded to Siemens, the integrated schedule baseline had been approved, and an April 2023 schedule update still showed the project on schedule. [meeting_doc_113971#p033_c0003] The project table kept Q2 2026 substantial completion and $542.6 million budget, with E-J Electric 28 percent complete and Mitsubishi 26 percent complete. [meeting_doc_113971#p014_c0017] A June 2023 signals-plan amendment also summarized QBL East as on schedule with Q2 2026 as both the last-report and forecast date. [meeting_doc_114211#p011_c0002]

By March 2024, the status table still showed Q2 2026 substantial completion, a $540.3 million budget, the installer 36 percent complete, and Mitsubishi 40 percent complete. [meeting_doc_135341#p012_c0005] A program summary said QBL East was progressing on schedule and within budget, while the IEC budget table separately showed a $540.3 million current budget, $542 million IEC EAC, and a projected $2 million soft-cost shortfall. [meeting_doc_135341#p016_c0008] [meeting_doc_135341#p023_c0003] [meeting_doc_135341#p023_c0004]

By July 2025, the committee update had shifted to Q2 2027 in-service, Q3 2027 substantial completion, a $540 million budget, E-J Electric 70 percent complete, and Mitsubishi 69 percent complete. [meeting_doc_179731#p015_c0006] The update said field work, including special trackwork, had made significant progress, but delays remained tied to finalizing the Book of Plans for the legacy signaling system; the first interlocking in-service was scheduled for Q4 2025, and Mitsubishi CBTC in-services were still planned for 2026. [meeting_doc_179731#p015_c0010]

The July 2025 IEC section was more pessimistic than the committee update. It said the integrated contractual substantial-completion date had converged at July 2026, the project forecast had moved to July 2027, and the IEC forecast was March 2028 for integrated substantial completion and August 2027 for CBTC in-service. [meeting_doc_179731#p043_c0004] [meeting_doc_179731#p043_c0006] The IEC budget page listed a $542 million budget, $540 million project EAC, $568 million IEC EAC, and concluded that the current budget was insufficient to support the project through substantial completion. [meeting_doc_179731#p044_c0003] [meeting_doc_179731#p044_c0005] [meeting_doc_179731#p044_c0006]

## Delivery And Risk

Recurring risks center on ATS expansion, software releases, system interfaces, and track access. In 2022, Siemens was still developing the ATS expansion tool that Mitsubishi needed for QBL East ATS database work, and the contractor had not provided a detailed software-release plan for C&D to manage testing and deployment. [meeting_doc_100281#p041_c0003] [meeting_doc_100281#p041_c0005] The same IEC review flagged Mitsubishi's need to finalize software interfaces with SSIs and Siemens/Thales onboard equipment. [meeting_doc_100281#p042_c0003]

In June 2023, the ATS extension tool had slipped ten months to November 2023; Siemens was providing interim deliverables, but the IEC warned that further delay could affect the project schedule. [meeting_doc_113971#p036_c0003] Mitsubishi's technical progress was otherwise described positively, including a Zone Controller Factory Acceptance Test in Japan with no failing test cases, though the IEC also flagged Zone Controller capacity as a schedule and budget risk because the controllers initially handled up to 24 trains, below peak demand. [meeting_doc_135341#p047_c0003]

By July 2025, some earlier technical concerns had improved: Mitsubishi upgraded Zone Controller CPUs to support 30 trains, secured Integrated Test Facility access, and the 8 Av axle-counter manual-reset process had been finalized for implementation on QBL East. [meeting_doc_179731#p046_c0003] The active risk picture had shifted toward field access, Book of Plans production, and coordination: the IEC said high demand for TA-provided flaggers, work trains, and diversions had become a realized schedule risk, and that the Siemens ATS Extension Tool remained critical to Mitsubishi database development, testing, and in-service. [meeting_doc_179731#p045_c0003] [meeting_doc_179731#p045_c0005]
