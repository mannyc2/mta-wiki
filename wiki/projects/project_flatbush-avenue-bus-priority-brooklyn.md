## Overview

Flatbush Avenue Bus Priority began as part of a broader NYC DOT/MTA effort to improve bus service: the June 2022 kickoff materials say the Transit Improvement Summit launched planning and outreach for the next round of bus priority projects, including Flatbush Avenue, alongside goals for new/enhanced bus lanes, automated enforcement, transit signal priority, and access to transit [flatbush_ave_brooklyn_bus_priority_kick_off_june2022#p004_c0002] [flatbush_ave_brooklyn_bus_priority_kick_off_june2022#p004_c0003]. The corridor-wide problem statement was broad: Flatbush was described as a critical cross-borough transportation corridor with 118,000 average daily bus riders on 12 routes in 2019, slow bus speeds, and Vision Zero safety concerns [flatbush_ave_brooklyn_bus_priority_kick_off_june2022#p006_c0002].

The initial corridor framing covered Flatbush Avenue between Tillary Street and Avenue V, while the current design work narrowed first to the Downtown Brooklyn/Northern Flatbush section because it had the slowest bus speeds and about 69,000 average daily bus riders on six MTA routes [better_buses#p001_b0001] [flatbush_ave_cab2_jun2024#p010_c0002]. By June 2025, DOT described the focus segment as Livingston Street to Grand Army Plaza and said future phases would study other sections of the corridor [flatbush_ave_cb8_jun2025#p009_c0002].

## Need And Riders

The corridor serves many riders who depend on buses and walking. A 2025 deck states that 132,000 daily riders use 12 bus routes on Flatbush Avenue, with bus speeds sometimes as slow as 4 mph; it also says 59% of households lack access to a private vehicle, and cites worker impacts from bus delays [flatbush_ave_cb8_jun2025#p004_c0002]. Earlier project materials describe the broader corridor area as having 985,000 residents, 59% of households without private-vehicle access, and 76% commuting by transit, walking, or biking [flatbush_ave_brooklyn_bus_priority_kick_off_june2022#p008_c0003].

B41 is the spine route in the project narrative. The kickoff deck identifies the B41 as the primary Flatbush Avenue route, with local and limited service from Kings Plaza/Bergen Beach to Downtown Brooklyn, and lists secondary routes such as the B9, B67, B103, Q35, and BM2 [flatbush_ave_brooklyn_bus_priority_kick_off_june2022#p011_c0002]. The same materials say buses move slowly northbound between Atlantic Avenue and Cadman Plaza, with AM peak trips taking five minutes longer than overnight trips because of congestion and double parking [flatbush_ave_brooklyn_bus_priority_kick_off_june2022#p012_c0002].

## Design Direction

In 2024, DOT was still comparing curbside, offset, and center-running bus lane options, with stated goals to improve bus speeds and reliability, improve pedestrian safety, and study traffic effects [flatbush_ave_cab2_jun2024#p030_c0003]. The later proposal selected center-running bus lanes. DOT's rationale was that existing left-turn bans made center-running unusually feasible on Flatbush Avenue; the design would put physically separated bus space in the middle of the roadway, use bus boarding islands for pedestrian refuge and near-level boarding, reduce bus-vehicle conflicts and lane blockages, and add public-realm benefits [flatbush_ave_bus_priority_sept2025#p006_c0002].

The bus stop plan is a major operational piece. In October 2025, DOT/MTA said they were evaluating routing with the Brooklyn Bus Network Redesign, proposed removing six of 12 curbside bus stops and consolidating service onto six boarding islands, concentrated at Atlantic Avenue/Barclays Center, Bergen Street, and 7th Avenue [flatbush_ave_bus_priority_cb6_oct2025#p011_c0002]. The same block estimates that a 20% speed improvement from center-running bus lanes would save about two minutes per passenger trip, or 11,675 passenger-minutes per day on the B41 local and limited [flatbush_ave_bus_priority_cb6_oct2025#p011_c0002].

## Street And Curb Changes

The proposal pairs bus lanes with curb and pedestrian changes. A September 2025 deck proposes weekday truck loading, metered parking, no-standing windows, and new side-street metered parking/loading on Pacific Street, Dean Street, Bergen Street, St. Marks Avenue, Prospect Place, and Park Place; it says the project would remove 41 net parking spaces while changing curb management to support Flatbush businesses [flatbush_ave_bus_priority_sept2025#p013_c0002]. The October 2025 summary lists center-running bus lanes, high-quality stops, 15,800 square feet of painted pedestrian space, 13 daylighted/shortened crossings, 13,000+ square feet of concrete pedestrian space via six boarding islands, up to 10 roadway bike-parking areas for 150+ bikes, and 17 loading zones fitting 70+ trucks or 112 passenger vehicles [flatbush_ave_bus_priority_cb6_oct2025#p027_c0002].

By April 2026, the project limits for the active MTP phase were described as Flatbush Avenue from State Street to Plaza Street West, the Grand Army Plaza oval, and a set of connecting streets around Atlantic Avenue, Pacific Street, 4th Avenue, Dean Street, Bergen Street, 5th Avenue, and nearby side streets [flatbush_ave_bus_priority_mtp_briefing_apr2026#p019_c0001] [flatbush_ave_bus_priority_mtp_briefing_apr2026#p019_c0002]. The April 2026 design summary includes center-running physically protected bus lanes, one general travel lane in each direction between Atlantic Avenue and Grand Army Plaza, two general lanes north of 4th/Atlantic, painted pedestrian spaces, updated parking/loading, a Pacific Street reversal between Flatbush and 4th Avenue, and a large-vehicle right-turn ban from southbound Flatbush to westbound Bergen [flatbush_ave_bus_priority_mtp_briefing_apr2026#p007_c0002].

## Implementation Status In The Repo Evidence

The latest source reviewed here is the April 2026 MTP briefing. It says DOT presented the center-running design in fall 2025, split implementation into two phases, installed center-running bus lanes between Livingston Street and State Street in fall 2025, and planned to install center-running bus lanes, bus boarding islands, new pedestrian spaces, and parking/loading updates between State Street and Grand Army Plaza in spring 2026 [flatbush_ave_bus_priority_mtp_briefing_apr2026#p004_c0002]. Its projected phasing shows concrete and signal construction beginning in spring 2026, construction and final markings in summer/fall 2026, bus lanes opening in late fall 2026, and monitoring/adjustment through fall 2027 [flatbush_ave_bus_priority_mtp_briefing_apr2026#p017_c0003] [flatbush_ave_bus_priority_mtp_briefing_apr2026#p017_c0004] [flatbush_ave_bus_priority_mtp_briefing_apr2026#p017_c0005] [flatbush_ave_bus_priority_mtp_briefing_apr2026#p017_c0006] [flatbush_ave_bus_priority_mtp_briefing_apr2026#p017_c0007] [flatbush_ave_bus_priority_mtp_briefing_apr2026#p017_c0008] [flatbush_ave_bus_priority_mtp_briefing_apr2026#p017_c0009] [flatbush_ave_bus_priority_mtp_briefing_apr2026#p017_c0010].
