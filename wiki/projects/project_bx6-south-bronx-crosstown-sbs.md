## Overview

Bx6 South Bronx Crosstown Select Bus Service was framed as a Select Bus Service project for the Bx6 corridor serving the South Bronx and Upper Manhattan. The November 2015 kickoff deck identified the corridor as a candidate from the 2009 Bus Rapid Transit Phase II study, serving nearly 25,000 daily riders and connecting to subway lines, Metro-North, and 20 bus routes including Bx41 SBS. Early maps and delay summaries placed the planning corridor between Washington Heights/Riverside Drive and Hunts Point, with the route crossing the Harlem River and serving places such as Yankee Stadium, Melrose Avenue, Westchester Avenue, Hunts Point Avenue, and the Hunts Point Cooperative Market [2015_11_20_brt_south_bronx_crosstown_kickoff#p001_c0001] [2015_11_20_brt_south_bronx_crosstown_kickoff#p003_c0002] [2015_11_20_brt_south_bronx_crosstown_kickoff#p004_c0002] [2015_11_20_brt_south_bronx_crosstown_kickoff#p005_c0002] [bx6_spring_newsletter_2017#p001_c0015].

## Why SBS Was Proposed

The project's need statement combined long travel times, boarding delay, bottlenecks, and safety concerns. The kickoff deck reported 56 minutes to travel 4.8 miles from Hunts Point to Riverside Drive, with more than 25% of the trip spent boarding passengers and the bus moving less than half the time; the same page's delay breakdown showed westbound weekday PM trips in motion 41% of the time, at bus stops 27%, at red lights 23%, and at crawl speed 8%. Other early challenges included Macombs Dam Bridge congestion, courthouse-area congestion with placard/illegal parking, and Vision Zero priority locations on 161st Street and Hunts Point Avenue [2015_11_20_brt_south_bronx_crosstown_kickoff#p006_c0002] [2015_11_20_brt_south_bronx_crosstown_kickoff#p006_c0003] [2015_11_20_brt_south_bronx_crosstown_kickoff#p007_c0002] [2015_11_20_brt_south_bronx_crosstown_kickoff#p008_c0002] [2015_11_20_brt_south_bronx_crosstown_kickoff#p011_c0002].

## Planning And Outreach

By the mid-2016 presentations, the outreach record included a Bronx kickoff meeting in November 2015, on-street outreach at six AM-peak locations from November 2015 through June 2016, more than 1,300 Bx6 SBS information cards distributed in English and Spanish, 578 individual comments, an online feedback portal, elected-official briefings, and Bronx and Manhattan community board meetings. Later 2017 materials added traffic and pedestrian data collection, small stakeholder meetings, Bronx Borough President meetings, and additional community board/elected-official discussions as the design moved toward implementation [brt_bx6_south_bronx_june2016#p008_c0002] [brt_bx6_south_bronx_sept2016#p008_c0002] [brt_bx6_cb4_mar2017#p008_c0002] [brt_bx6_south_bronx_mar2017#p008_c0002].

## Service And Street Design

The proposed service plan split the existing Bx6 into two related services: Bx6 SBS would stop only at SBS stops, while Bx6 local would continue stopping at every or nearly every current stop, with frequencies split between the SBS and local services. The fare-payment concept also separated the services operationally: local fares would be paid on the bus, while SBS fares would be paid on the street at fare machines; the Spring 2017 newsletter explained off-board fare payment as paying before boarding and then boarding through any door [brt_bx6_south_bronx_cb10_june2016#p013_c0002] [brt_bx6_south_bronx_cb10_june2016#p018_c0002] [brt_bx6_cb4_mar2017#p010_c0003] [bx6_spring_newsletter_2017#p001_c0004].

The street-design package paired SBS operations with targeted physical changes. General SBS features in the Bx6 decks included bus lanes, signal priority, off-board fare collection, all-door boarding, station amenities, real-time passenger information, and pedestrian-safety improvements. The Spring 2017 newsletter said NYC DOT and MTA had chosen key locations for bus lanes and boarding islands, described dedicated eastbound bus-only lanes under the Grand Concourse, and listed real-time arrival information, off-board fare collection, and wider sidewalks as part of the waiting and pedestrian environment [brt_bx6_south_bronx#p004_c0002] [brt_bx6_south_bronx#p004_c0006] [brt_bx6_south_bronx#p004_c0007] [brt_bx6_south_bronx#p004_c0011] [brt_bx6_south_bronx#p005_c0004] [brt_bx6_south_bronx#p005_c0006] [brt_bx6_south_bronx#p005_c0007] [brt_bx6_south_bronx#p005_c0009] [bx6_spring_newsletter_2017#p001_c0015] [bx6_spring_newsletter_2017#p001_c0017] [bx6_spring_newsletter_2017#p001_c0019] [bx6_spring_newsletter_2017#p001_c0021].

## 161st Street Focus

The 161st Street/Grand Concourse area became a major design focus, but within these sources it is part of the 2015-2017 Bx6 SBS planning thread rather than a standalone reconstruction project. March 2017 project goals were to improve bus speeds, address boarding and vehicle-organization issues in front of the courts, improve pedestrian conditions, and maintain/better organize traffic flow. The same materials documented narrow sidewalks west of the tunnel, 38 pedestrian injuries at 161st Street and River Avenue from 2010-2014, curb-access problems at Sherman Avenue and Concourse Village West, and a final one-way bus-tunnel proposal described as beneficial for 25,000 daily bus riders, with no significant traffic impacts and with the boarding issue in front of the courts addressed [brt_bx6_cb4_mar2017#p007_c0002] [brt_bx6_cb4_mar2017#p017_c0003] [brt_bx6_cb4_mar2017#p018_c0003] [brt_bx6_cb4_mar2017#p019_c0002] [brt_bx6_cb4_mar2017#p019_c0003] [brt_bx6_south_bronx_mar2017#p033_c0002].

## Timeline And Scope

The documented schedule shifted as planning advanced. A June 2016 Manhattan CB10 deck listed continued outreach, draft street design, and traffic analysis in spring 2016; final street design and service plan in summer/fall 2016; and launch of Bx6 SBS service in spring 2017. The Spring 2017 newsletter's timeline instead showed planning/design beginning in fall 2015, implementation/construction and service planning in spring 2017, and service starting in summer 2017. March 2017 materials likewise listed fare-machine installation and street-design implementation in spring 2017, launch in summer 2017, and a 2018-2020 capital-project design/implementation phase [brt_bx6_south_bronx_cb10_june2016#p022_c0002] [bx6_spring_newsletter_2017#p002_c0010] [brt_bx6_cb4_mar2017#p040_c0002] [brt_bx6_south_bronx_mar2017#p040_c0002].

For this page, the clean scope is the 2015-2017 SBS planning and initial implementation program for the Bx6 South Bronx Crosstown corridor. Later post-launch Bx6 project updates separately describe Bx6 Select Bus Service as launched on September 3, 2017, with implemented bus lanes, fare machines, boarding islands, and the eastbound 161st Street bus-only tunnel; a separate 2022 record treats 161st Street Reconstruction as full reconstruction of 161st Street from River Avenue to Park Avenue [brt_bx6_cb2_edc_may2022#p004_c0002] [brt_bx6_cb2_edc_may2022#p005_c0001] [brt_bx6_cb2_edc_may2022#p005_c0002] [brt_bx6_cb2_edc_may2022#p005_c0003] [brt_bx6_cb2_edc_may2022#p005_c0004] [brt_bx6_cb2_edc_may2022#p005_c0005] [brt_bx6_cb4_may2022#p016_c0001] [brt_bx6_cb4_may2022#p016_c0002] [brt_bx6_cb4_may2022#p016_c0003].

## Caveats

Launch timing differs by source: mid-2016 sources say spring 2017, while Spring/March 2017 sources say summer 2017; September 3, 2017 appears in later 2022 post-launch records. Several Bx6 project records remain partially overlapping in the repo. The synthesis above treats the target page as the merged pre-launch SBS project and leaves later capital-update/performance material to the separate Bx6 SBS and 161st Street pages.

The May 2022 CB2/EDC update treats Bx6 SBS as an already-launched service, with launch dated
Sept. 3, 2017, and a post-launch capital-improvement phase. The implemented package included bus
lanes, fare machines, boarding islands, and the eastbound 161st Street Bus Only Tunnel.
