## 2025 Proposal Context

In identity terms, this record is adjacent to, but not the same as, the separate 34th Street Busway record. The busway source defines a busway running eastbound from Ninth Avenue to Third Avenue and westbound from Third Avenue to Ninth Avenue, while the 2025 Enhanced Bus Priority sources define a broader 34th Street study area from 12th Avenue to FDR Drive and then focus the draft busway treatment between 3rd Avenue and 9th Avenue. [34th_st_busway#p002_c0002] [34th_st_busway#p002_c0003] [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb6_jun2025#p004_c0002] [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb6_jun2025#p010_c0002]

The 2025 decks also treat earlier 34th Street bus work as background, not as the new proposal itself. They list curbside bus lanes added in 2008, the M34/M34-A SBS launch in 2011, upgrades from curbside to offset bus lanes, eleven bus bulbs, 2024 on-street engagement, and January-February 2025 existing-conditions presentations before saying the meeting is presenting a draft proposal. That makes the page distinct from the older 34th Street SBS capital/service record and from the earlier Transitway concept, which described a two-way Transitway for the length of 34th Street aligned along the curb. [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb6_jun2025#p007_c0002] [120418_brt_cb4_34th_street#p003_c0002] [120418_brt_cb4_34th_street#p022_c0001] [100503_brt_cb5_34th_street_transitway#p014_c0003]

## Why DOT Studied 34th Street

The 2025 project rationale combines transit, pedestrian, freight, and curb demands. DOT described 34th Street as a 12th Avenue-to-FDR Drive study area with 28,000 daily bus passengers on the M34/M34-A SBS and express buses, slow crosstown bus speeds, connections to subway, PATH, LIRR, NJ Transit, and Amtrak, numerous local and express bus routes, heavy commercial and pedestrian activity, and Vision Zero corridor status. [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb6_jun2025#p004_c0002]

The bus-speed case is especially central. The May and June proposal decks say bus speeds improved after the launch of Select Bus Service, but that M34/M34A SBS remained one of the slowest buses, with average weekday peak-hour speeds of 5.0 mph and segments as low as 3 mph. They also state that roadway congestion causes more than 750 hours of passenger delay on the M34/M34A+ on an average weekday. [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb6_jun2025#p006_c0003]

The safety case is tied to crash history on the corridor. The January deck says more than 450 people were injured in crashes on 34th Street from 2019 to 2023, averaging more than seven injuries a month, and identifies 34th Street as a Vision Zero Priority Corridor with priority intersections at 11th Avenue, 8th Avenue, 6th Avenue/Broadway, and FDR Drive. The injury table records 466 total injuries, 32 severe injuries, one fatality, and 33 KSI over the five-year period. [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb5_jan2025#p011_c0002] [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb5_jan2025#p011_c0004]

## Outreach And Draft Proposal

The proposal responds to both public and merchant feedback. DOT reported pedestrian intercept surveys in September and October 2024, with more than 350 on-street conversations and 200 surveys, and a November 2024 merchant survey in which DOT visited every business on 34th Street from 9th Avenue to 2nd Avenue and received more than 100 participating businesses. The May/June decks summarize feedback as pedestrian concerns about sidewalk overcrowding, long crossings, and congestion; bus-rider concerns about slow speeds and long waits; business needs for additional loading space; and community-board requests for a 24/7 14th Street-style busway, sidewalk widening or other pedestrian safety elements, and delivery-supportive regulations. [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb5_jan2025#p014_c0002] [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb5_jan2025#p015_c0002] [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb6_jun2025#p008_c0002]

The draft proposal is a busway between 3rd Avenue and 9th Avenue in both directions, with existing bus lanes maintained on the rest of the corridor. The same slide lists new pedestrian safety improvements, specifically painted pedestrian spaces, hardened daylighting, and a concrete median island, plus curb management for loading and delivery needs. [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb6_jun2025#p010_c0002]

Under the busway rules described in the decks, buses, trucks, emergency vehicles, and Access-a-Ride could travel along the entire busway corridor. Other vehicles, including cars, taxis, and for-hire vehicles, would retain local access from side streets but would have to leave at the next required turn using posted signage. DOT framed the busway as a way to make bus service faster and more dependable while allowing loading, deliveries, and customer access to businesses. [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb6_jun2025#p011_c0002]

The concept includes detailed intersection operations rather than only a corridor-level line. The June deck diagrams gateway and avenue rules at 3rd Avenue, Lexington Avenue, Park Avenue, Madison Avenue, 5th Avenue, 6th Avenue/Broadway, 7th Avenue, 8th Avenue, and 9th Avenue, with required turns or existing turn restrictions for general traffic and continued movements for buses or trucks at key points. It also shows mid-block crossing upgrades between 9th and 6th Avenues, including painted pedestrian space to shorten crossings. ## Open Work At Proposal Time

The May/June decks present enforcement and analysis as active pieces of the proposal. They say ACE on-bus and fixed-location cameras already enforced illegal parking in bus stops, double parking, and required-turn violations on the 34th Street M34/M34A SBS route since August 10, 2020; for new regulations, cameras would issue warnings only for the first 60 days, with existing regulations enforced as usual and close coordination with NYPD for launch. [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb6_jun2025#p023_c0002]

Traffic analysis was still underway in the proposal decks. DOT said it had collected existing traffic and pedestrian volumes and developed an existing-conditions model, while ongoing work included proposed-condition models, analysis of traffic-pattern effects, and signal-timing exploration. The planned traffic-analysis report would use dynamic traffic modeling for Manhattan avenues and crosstown streets between 66th Street and 14th Street, with results to be provided after completion. [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb6_jun2025#p025_c0002]

The source-time status is therefore draft and pending, not proven implementation. The June summary lists goals to make bus service faster and more reliable, increase corridor safety, and ensure access to businesses; it summarizes the proposal as a 14th Street-style busway between 3rd Avenue and 9th Avenue; and it lists ongoing work as finalizing traffic analysis, further developing the design, and synthesizing community feedback. The next-step slide says feedback would shape DOT's final proposal, traffic analysis would continue, a refined plan would be shared in summer 2025, and implementation was projected for summer/fall 2025. [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb6_jun2025#p031_c0002] [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb6_jun2025#p032_c0003] [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb6_jun2025#p032_c0005] [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb6_jun2025#p032_c0007] [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb6_jun2025#p032_c0008]
