## Scope

This record covers the 34th Street Transitway as a planning-era BRT concept and selected alternative, not the later implemented 34th Street SBS capital project or the 2025-era busway/enhanced bus-priority proposal. In the 2008 concept material, the Transitway was shown as a dedicated two-way facility for the full length of 34th Street, with stations from the West Side/Javits Center area through the East 34th Street Ferry Terminal area. The same 2008 presentation also framed it as a long-term concept: a river-to-river, fully separated, two-way transitway on one side of 34th Street, with the remaining right-of-way potentially used for one-way traffic, new plaza and pedestrian space, and reserved commercial loading space [080421_brt_cb5_34th_street_fullboardpresentation#p016_c0001] [080421_brt_cb5_34th_street_fullboardpresentation#p017_c0003].

## Why It Was Studied

The Transitway was advanced out of an alternatives-analysis process focused on crosstown transit operations, express bus operations, pedestrian congestion, and future development. A March 2010 presentation described 17,000 daily local-bus passengers, 16,000 daily express-bus passengers, slow bus service despite existing bus lanes, and bus travel time divided among motion, dwell time, and signal delay. The same alternatives process considered No Build, Transportation System Management, Bus Rapid Transit, Light Rail Transit, Automated Guideway Transit, Heavy Rail, and Streetcar options [100331_brt_cb5_34th_street_transitway#p003_c0002] [100331_brt_cb5_34th_street_transitway#p006_c0001] [100331_brt_cb5_34th_street_transitway#p006_c0011].

The selected-alternative finding was explicit: the BRT alternative, labeled 34th Street Transitway, scored higher than the No Build and TSM alternatives in secondary screening and was identified as the selected alternative. In later September 2010 timeline material, the project history was summarized as PlaNYC's 2007 BRT emphasis, 34th Street's identification as a transit-need corridor, short-term bus-lane improvements in 2008, and the 2010 selection of the Transitway as the Locally Preferred Alternative [100331_brt_cb5_34th_street_transitway#p010_c0002] [100331_brt_cb5_34th_street_transitway#p010_c0003] [100915_brt_cb5_34th_street_transitway#p006_c0002].

## Design Concept

The selected Transitway concept put two-way bus operations along the curb for the length of 34th Street. The March and May 2010 presentations explain that a single-side alignment was chosen because the street was too narrow for a center-running transitway while also preserving parking on both sides. In that version, the transitway occupied about half of the street, with the north half east of Fifth Avenue, the south half west/east of Sixth Avenue depending on source rendering, and a plaza between Fifth and Sixth Avenues; general traffic was described as one-way eastbound east of Fifth Avenue and one-way westbound west of Sixth Avenue [100331_brt_cb5_34th_street_transitway#p013_c0003] [100503_brt_cb5_34th_street_transitway#p014_c0003].

The concept bundled several BRT-style treatments: high-quality bus lanes, fare prepayment, transit signal priority, real-time information, pedestrian-safety changes, and protected or enhanced bus lanes for the full length of 34th Street. September 2010 materials list transit signal priority at many intersections, proof-of-payment fare pre-payment, real-time information, curb extensions, crosswalk median refuges, boarding islands raised for near-level boarding, and enhanced full-corridor bus lanes [100331_brt_cb5_34th_street_transitway#p011_c0002] [100915_brt_cb5_34th_street_transitway#p009_c0002] [100915_brt_cb5_34th_street_transitway#p009_c0005] [100915_brt_cb5_34th_street_transitway#p010_c0004] [100915_brt_cb5_34th_street_transitway#p011_c0003].

## Outreach, Curb Access, And Review

The Transitway record is also a curb-access and environmental-review record. The first CAC meeting in June 2010 raised design and traffic-flow issues including commuter and tour-bus use, Lincoln Tunnel traffic, parking garages, 33rd Street circulation, pedestrian space, enforcement, project funding, and fare collection. DOT responses in that meeting said garage access would be maintained, pedestrian space was a major priority, traffic analysis would examine effects, planning work was funded by a federal CMAQ grant, and additional federal construction grants had been applied for but were not yet approved [20100615_34th_transitway_cac_mtg_summary#p001_c0015] [20100615_34th_transitway_cac_mtg_summary#p002_c0007] [20100615_34th_transitway_cac_mtg_summary#p002_c0011] [20100615_34th_transitway_cac_mtg_summary#p003_c0005].

By fall 2010 and winter 2011, the design work had become highly block-specific. DOT collected curbside-activity information through four October/November 2010 community forums, surveys, building-manager outreach, field observations on 34th, 33rd, and 35th Streets, taxi GPS data, and trucking-industry meetings. The stated purpose was to guide preliminary design, including loading and parking-zone locations and Transitway access rules, with public sharing planned for spring 2011 [34th_st_curbside_activity#p001_c0003] [34th_st_curbside_activity#p001_c0005] [34th_st_curbside_activity#p013_c0005].

The January 2011 CAC materials show the project still in design and review rather than implementation. The schedule called for preliminary design and traffic-analysis presentations in spring 2011, revised design and a draft Environmental Assessment in summer 2011, and a final EA/project determination in fall 2011. The environmental review materials said DOT had applied for FTA funding, had to comply with NEPA, SEQRA, and CEQR, and would analyze transportation, parking and curb access, transit travel times and reliability, bus-route changes, ridership and mode choice, and pedestrian conditions [20110118_34th_cac3_slides#p005_c0002] [20110118_34th_cac3_slides#p022_c0002] [20110118_34th_cac3_slides#p026_c0002].

## Relationship To Later 34th Street Bus Projects

Later repo evidence should not be folded back into this Transitway concept as if it were the same delivered project. Separate 2011-2016 SBS records describe 2011 planned improvements such as off-board fare collection and bus-lane camera enforcement, plus a 2012 proposed SBS design with offset bus lanes, bus bulbs, sidewalk extensions, and expanded loading zones; by 2016, the SBS capital project had completed construction from 12th Avenue to Lexington Avenue, with the Lexington-to-FDR section expected later that year [201110_brt_34th_open_house_slides#p005_c0002] [brt_34thst_sbs_newsletter3_winter_2016#p002_c0005] [brt_34thst_sbs_newsletter3_winter_2016#p002_c0013].

The later busway/enhanced bus-priority materials are also distinct. Current busway materials describe a 34th Street Busway between Ninth Avenue and Third Avenue, in effect daily from 6am to 10pm, with through access limited to buses, qualifying trucks, Access-A-Ride, and emergency vehicles, while passenger vehicles must make the next available right turn. The 2025 enhanced bus-priority proposal likewise studies 34th Street from 12th Avenue to FDR Drive and proposes a 14th Street-style busway between Third and Ninth Avenues while maintaining existing bus lanes elsewhere [busway_34thstreet#p001_b0001] [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb6_jun2025#p004_c0002] [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb6_jun2025#p010_c0002].

## Caveats

The materialized project identity merges concept, CAC, curbside-activity, and selected-alternative records. The synthesis above treats them as one planning-era Transitway record, but not as proof of implementation. The 2010 sources disagree slightly in wording around whether the Transitway occupied the south half east or west of Sixth Avenue; this page preserves that ambiguity rather than resolving it. Repo evidence checked here supports later SBS and busway records as separate project identities; it does not show that the full two-way river-to-river Transitway concept was built as proposed.
