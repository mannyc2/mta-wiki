## Scope

This page is about the 2011/2012 34th Street Select Bus Service implementation for the M34/M34A corridor: the service change that renamed the M34 as M34 SBS, renamed the M16 as M34A SBS, and introduced pre-payment at all M34 and M34A stops on Sunday, November 13, 2011. It should be kept distinct from the earlier 34th Street Transitway/BRT Alternative, which the alternatives analysis described as a transitway-based LPA, and from later 2025 Enhanced Bus Priority planning for a busway between Third Avenue and Ninth Avenue [201110_brt_34th_open_house_slides#p028_c0002] [34thstreet_aa_final#p012_c0006] [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb5_may2025#p010_c0002].

The January 2012 Project Analysis Report framed the proposed project as a collaboration among NYCDOT, MTA New York City Transit, and MTA Bus Company to implement enhanced bus service along 34th Street from the Hudson River to the East River in Manhattan. That broader river-to-river framing is the best source-backed way to understand the project corridor, while individual design and construction slides break the work into section-specific pieces [201201_brt_34th_project_analysis_report#p007_c0003] [201201_brt_34th_project_analysis_report#p013_c0004].

## Service Launch And Design

The fall 2011 SBS materials separated the project into near-term service changes and a 2012 street-design package. The 2011 planned improvements were off-board fare collection and bus lane camera enforcement; the 2012 proposed design included offset bus lanes, bus bulbs and sidewalk extensions, and expanded loading zones [201110_brt_34th_open_house_slides#p005_c0002] [2011_brt_34th_cb6_slides#p005_c0002].

The fare-payment change was operationally specific: riders were instructed to pay before boarding at a sidewalk MetroCard or coin machine, take a proof-of-payment receipt, and board through the front or rear door without showing the receipt to the driver. The same launch slide said service levels and routes would remain the same on both M34 SBS and M34A SBS, even as the route names and payment process changed [201110_brt_34th_open_house_slides#p030_c0002] [201110_brt_34th_open_house_slides#p028_c0002].

The planning case for the 2012 design emphasized bus reliability and speed for more than 33,000 daily riders, 18,000 square feet of new pedestrian space, a daytime loading-space increase from 32 to 258 spaces, standard bus and pedestrian design elements, emergency-vehicle use of improved bus lanes, and two-way river-to-river traffic [2011_brt_34th_cb6_slides#p010_c0002].

Traffic and curb-management materials tried to answer the obvious tradeoff questions. The build traffic analysis stated that overall traffic delays would remain roughly the same, that some intersections would be slightly better and others slightly worse, that diversion to 35th Street would be small, and that the Manhattan Traffic Model showed no effect beyond the project area. A public-comment response also said curb regulations would prioritize loading over parking in consultation with the CAC, residents, and stakeholders [2011_brt_34th_cb6_slides#p021_c0002] [2011_34th_sbs_comments_on_proposed_design#p003_c0013].

## 2012 Status And Phasing

By the March 28, 2012 CAC #6 update, DOT and NYCT reported that off-board fare collection had been implemented, environmental review had been completed, the Project Analysis Report had been released, and design work had begun. The same update said the next steps were to finalize the implementation schedule, complete design, and begin construction [201203_brt_34th_cac6#p001_c0001] [201203_brt_34th_cac6#p001_c0002] [201203_brt_34th_cac6#p003_c0002].

Early operating results were reported alongside that status update: travel times were reduced by 10%, ridership of 20,200 was recorded on March 15, and NYCT planned April frequency increases of 24 weekday trips, 24 Saturday trips, and 12 Sunday trips with no additional cost to the MTA. The update also described MTA Bus Time as a real-time bus location system implemented on M34, available by web, text, and smartphone, replacing the earlier Clever Devices 34th Street pilot [201203_brt_34th_cac6#p004_c0002] [201203_brt_34th_cac6#p005_c0002].

Construction was presented in three named sections. The West Section was part of the Hudson Park and Boulevard Project, constructed by Hudson Yards Development Corporation, with bus lanes, loading zones, and 2 bus bulbs scheduled from June 2012 to July 2013. The Middle Section was to be constructed by the City of New York with New York City Transit, with bus lanes, loading zones, and 9 bus bulbs scheduled from August 2012 to April 2013. The East Section was to be constructed by the City of New York, combining DEP water/sewer work with DOT bus lanes, loading zones, and 3 bus bulbs, scheduled from summer 2013 to fall 2014 [201203_brt_34th_cac6#p020_c0002] [201203_brt_34th_cac6#p021_c0002] [201203_brt_34th_cac6#p024_c0002].

## Caveats

The materialized project identity merges the 2011 service launch, 2012 capital design, Phase II SBS evidence, and some later newsletter evidence. The synthesis above scopes this page to the M34/M34A SBS implementation, not the transitway LPA or 2025 busway planning. Corridor endpoints vary by source: river-to-river, Twelfth Avenue to First Avenue, and later Twelfth Avenue to FDR Drive all appear in related records and sources. The 2012 schedule projected completion through fall 2014, while the 2016 newsletter says 12th Avenue to Lexington Avenue was completed in 2015 and Lexington Avenue to FDR Service Road was expected by December 2016.
