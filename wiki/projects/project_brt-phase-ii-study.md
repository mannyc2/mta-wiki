## Study Role

The BRT Phase II Study was the citywide planning bridge between the first SBS/BRT deployments and a larger pipeline of future bus-priority corridors. DOT and MTA New York City Transit initiated it in fall 2008 after the Bx12 SBS launch, framing BRT as a cost-effective way to improve existing bus service while limiting NYCT operating-budget impacts; the study combined transit-needs analysis and public outreach and identified 16 potential future BRT corridors. [brt_phase2_future_corridors_rpt#p002_c0003]

The technical screen started with a citywide transit-needs analysis focused on underserved areas, slow transit trips, crowded subway lines, and future growth. [brt_phase2_future_corridors_rpt#p002_c0004] Public input then added seven workshops and an online survey in spring 2009, with over 325 workshop participants, representatives from 47 community and business groups, staff from 23 local elected officials' offices, and over 600 online survey responses. [brt_phase2_future_corridors_rpt#p002_c0006] A later public-input report summarized that outreach in June 2010. [brt_phase2_future_corridors_rpt#p002_c0007]

## Corridor Selection

DOT and NYCT compiled over 40 potential BRT corridors from the analysis and outreach, then evaluated them by community feedback, ridership potential, network benefits, bus lane or busway feasibility, and geographic diversity. [brt_phase2_future_corridors_rpt#p003_c0003] The resulting 16-corridor list was not itself an implementation program: the report says agencies would draw from it as funding, community support, and related transit projects allowed, with corridor-by-corridor advisory committees and public workshops during planning. [brt_phase2_future_corridors_rpt#p003_c0004]

The shortlist for the next planning effort was Webster Avenue/Third Avenue in the Bronx, Hillside Avenue in Queens, and LaGuardia/East Elmhurst in Queens. [brt_phase2_future_corridors_rpt#p003_c0005] The full candidate table also included South Bronx East-West, Utica Avenue, Bushwick to Downtown Brooklyn, Southern Brooklyn East-West, Flatbush Avenue, 125th Street Crosstown, Upper West Side/Upper East Side Crosstown, 14th Street Crosstown, Manhattan West Side, Southeast Queens, Flushing-Jamaica, Woodhaven Boulevard, and Manhattan-Northern Boulevard-Flushing. [brt_phase2_future_corridors_rpt#p004_c0002]

## Webster Avenue Follow-Through

The Webster Avenue CAC presentation shows how the study became a project pipeline source. In that 2012 Webster Avenue SBS context, the Phase II recommendations are described as based on 2009 workshops and technical analysis; Webster Avenue is specifically framed as the highest-ranked Bronx workshop corridor and the highest-priority Phase II corridor citywide. [2012_02_06_brt_webster_cac1#p012_c0002] The same deck grounds Webster planning in the existing Bx41 route, a 5.3-mile corridor with 125,000 residents within a quarter mile, 74% of households without a car, and 61% of residents commuting by transit. [2012_02_06_brt_webster_cac1#p013_c0003] Its stated project benefits included faster and more reliable service for 22,000 daily Bx41 riders, a larger Bronx SBS network, coordinated Webster/Third Avenue service patterns, safety improvements, and economic-development support. [2012_02_06_brt_webster_cac1#p024_c0002]

## Reading Notes

Treat "completed" here as completed study/report work, not as a claim that all Phase II corridors were built. The Phase II report describes future corridor selection as dependent on funding, community support, and related project progress, while the 2012 Webster deck is evidence of one corridor moving into SBS planning rather than evidence for every candidate corridor. [brt_phase2_future_corridors_rpt#p003_c0004] [2012_02_06_brt_webster_cac1#p012_c0002]
