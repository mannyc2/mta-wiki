## Corridor Role

Webster Avenue emerged from the BRT Phase II planning work as part of a broader Webster Avenue/Third Avenue Bronx transit need: the Phase II report identified underserved areas along the Webster Avenue/Third Avenue corridor, and its Bronx corridor table described existing bus services there as carrying a combined 69,000 trips a day. That early framing matters because the later Webster Avenue SBS project was not only a route upgrade; it was a north-south corridor intervention in an area where bus service carried much of the practical transit burden [brt_intro_to_phase2#p005_c0007] [brt_intro_to_phase2#p030_c0005].

By the 2012-2013 Webster planning materials, the corridor was defined around the existing Bx41 route, 5.3 miles from The Hub to Williamsbridge. The corridor materials described more than 20,000 daily Bx41 riders, 200,000 residents within a 10-minute walk, 71% of households without cars, and 61% of residents commuting by transit [2013_02_sbs_webster_bx_cb1#p007_c0002].

## Planning And Design

The problem statement combined bus delay, safety, and corridor growth. The 2014 progress report says pre-SBS Bx41 speeds were slow and unreliable because of traffic congestion, double parking, and high passenger boardings; it also says the pre-SBS Bx41 averaged seven miles per hour, spent nearly half its travel time stopped at bus stops or signals, and had one-way travel times varying by more than 20 minutes. Webster Avenue was also described as having one of the Bronx's highest vehicular accident rates, with 1,663 crashes, more than 2,000 injuries, and eight fatalities along the project corridor from 2006 to 2010 [2014_08_25_brt_websteravenue_progressreport#p005_c0004] [2014_08_25_brt_websteravenue_progressreport#p007_c0008] [2014_08_25_brt_websteravenue_progressreport#p007_c0009].

The design process used a Community Advisory Committee, public open houses, door-to-door business surveys, stakeholder meetings, and community-board presentations. The implementation plan converted Bx41 Limited service to Bx41 SBS and paired the route change with four miles of offset bus lanes, bus-bulb stations, off-board fare collection, low-floor buses, station and bus branding, transit signal priority, and pedestrian safety improvements [2013_02_sbs_webster_bx_cb1#p008_c0002] [2013_02_sbs_webster_bx_cb1#p011_c0002].

As a corridor design, the preferred package tried to balance bus priority with curb access and pedestrian safety. Webster materials stated that offset bus lanes would improve bus speed, bus bulbs would support higher-quality SBS stations, and the design would maintain curbside access and parking; they also described curb extensions and medians as pedestrian-safety improvements and traffic-flow tools [2012_11_sbs_webster_cac4#p012_c0002] [2012_11_sbs_webster_cac4#p013_c0002].

## Implementation And Street Changes

The implemented bus-lane segment was narrower than the full 5.3-mile corridor: the progress report says eight lane-miles of bus lanes, four miles in each direction, were installed on Webster Avenue from E 165 Street to E Gun Hill Road. Those lanes were painted terracotta red, signed overhead and curbside, and open to non-bus vehicles only for the next right turn, quick pickup/drop-off, or driveway/parking access; all buses on the corridor, including Bx41 Local buses, could use them [2014_08_25_brt_websteravenue_progressreport#p014_c0004] [2014_08_25_brt_websteravenue_progressreport#p014_c0005].

Pedestrian and street-design work was part of the corridor package, not a side item. In response to community support for pedestrian safety, NYCDOT installed nine pedestrian refuge islands and two longer medians, plus dedicated pedestrian space at E Tremont Avenue and E 205 Street/Parkside Place. Crews also resurfaced most of Webster Avenue between E 165 Street and E 204 Street and built accessible pedestrian ramps in multiple locations before launch [2014_08_25_brt_websteravenue_progressreport#p015_c0010] [2014_08_25_brt_websteravenue_progressreport#p016_c0005].

## Results And Later References

Bx41 SBS launched with the core SBS/street elements substantially in place on June 30, 2013. The progress report found that Bx41 SBS operated 19-23% faster than the former Bx41 Limited, while Bx41 Local also benefited from the Webster bus lanes and traffic improvements with 11-17% travel-time reductions. It also reported reduced bus travel times through key intersections including Fordham Road and the Cross Bronx Expressway [2014_08_25_brt_websteravenue_progressreport#p017_c0003] [2014_08_25_brt_websteravenue_progressreport#p018_c0004] [2014_08_25_brt_websteravenue_progressreport#p018_c0005].

The early results also point to corridor-level ridership shifts. Total Bx41 ridership increased nearly 25% after SBS launch, while ridership on the Third/Webster Avenue corridor between The Hub and Fordham Road increased more than 7%, or about 3,400 daily riders. The same report says the share of riders using Webster Avenue rather than Third Avenue between The Hub and Fordham Road rose from 38% to 44%, suggesting that the faster Webster trip shifted some corridor travel patterns [2014_08_25_brt_websteravenue_progressreport#p020_c0004] [2014_08_25_brt_websteravenue_progressreport#p020_c0007] [2014_08_25_brt_websteravenue_progressreport#p020_c0008].

Later records keep Webster in the bus-priority network. The 2017 TSP report lists Bx41 SBS/Webster Avenue as one of five new-generation TSP corridors implemented since 2012, with Bx41/Webster TSP limits from Third/Melrose Avenues to E Gun Hill/White Plains Roads; its before/after charts show peak-period Bx41 SBS travel-time reductions of roughly 17-21% by direction and period [tsp_report_2017#p005_c0007] [tsp_report_2017#p010_c0003] [tsp_report_2017#p010_c0004] [tsp_report_2017#p010_c0006] [tsp_report_2017#p010_c0007].

The corridor also reappears in later Better Buses work as a short southbound gap near the Cross Bronx Expressway. The 2019 plan identified Webster Avenue from E 176th Street to E 174th Street for a new southbound offset bus lane with a physical barrier and signal timing adjustments, because traffic approaching the Cross Bronx Expressway entrance blocked buses where the southbound bus lane stopped at E Tremont Avenue and did not resume until E 174th Street. A December 2019 CB5 update reported that the added southbound lane produced a 37% PM-peak bus-speed improvement and calmed turns onto the Cross Bronx Expressway [better_buses_action_plan_2019#p035_c0001] [better_buses_action_plan_2019#p035_c0008] [better_buses_action_plan_2019#p035_c0011] [bx_cb5_projects_dec032019#p003_c0004].

## Caveats

The materialized identity merges several scopes: Webster Avenue alone, Webster/Third Avenue, the Bx41 SBS corridor, the E 165 Street to E Gun Hill Road bus-lane segment, and the later E 176 Street to E 174 Street gap treatment. The synthesis above treats route-level Bx41 facts as corridor evidence only where the source ties them to Webster Avenue corridor performance. The repository still has duplicate or near-duplicate Webster corridor pages, so future identity review may change which page owns Webster/Third versus Webster-only context.
