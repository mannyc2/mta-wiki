## Corridor Context

Flatbush Avenue is framed in the repo evidence as a Brooklyn bus-priority and pedestrian-safety corridor, not just as the current northern construction segment. The broad NYC DOT/MTA project frame is Flatbush Avenue between Tillary Street and Avenue V, with the corridor identified as a future bus-priority corridor from the NYC/MTA Transit Improvement Summit, an NYC Streets Plan priority corridor, and a Vision Zero priority corridor [better_buses#p001_b0001]. The Brooklyn Bus Network draft plan likewise says NYC DOT is pursuing bus priority and pedestrian safety improvements between Tillary Street and Avenue V, citing more than 118,000 pre-pandemic daily bus riders on 12 routes, corridor-average bus speeds of 7 mph, and speeds below 5 mph in Downtown Brooklyn [brooklyn_bus_network_draft_plan_without_route_profiles#p022_c0005].

Early outreach materials describe Flatbush as a long cross-borough corridor linking Downtown Brooklyn to commercial hubs, cultural and educational institutions, and medical care. Those materials divide the corridor into three working sections: Tillary Street to Empire Boulevard, Empire Boulevard to Nostrand Avenue, and Nostrand Avenue to Kings Plaza, with different land uses, destinations, and roadway cross-sections in each section [flatbush_ave_brooklyn_bus_priority_kick_off_june2022#p009_c0002] [flatbush_ave_brooklyn_bus_priority_kick_off_june2022#p010_c0002]. That broader frame matters because later Northern Flatbush design work is repeatedly described as a first focus area rather than the entire corridor [flatbush_ave_cb8_jun2025#p009_c0002].

## Transit Role

Flatbush Avenue's transit role is both local and regional. A 2024 DOT deck calls it a major Brooklyn corridor connecting job centers, housing, entertainment, culture, shopping, and recreation, with Atlantic Terminal connections to LIRR and nine subway lines, 2/5 connections at Nostrand Junction, and B/Q/S connections at Prospect Park [flatbush_ave_cb8_jun2024#p004_c0003]. The B41 is the spine route in the source material, operating local and limited service from Kings Plaza/Bergen Beach to Downtown Brooklyn, while secondary routes such as the B9, B67, B103, Q35, and BM2 also use Flatbush Avenue for parts of their routes [flatbush_ave_brooklyn_bus_priority_kick_off_june2022#p011_c0002].

The corridor's rider counts and delay statements vary by source date and scope, but they consistently show a high-ridership, slow-bus corridor. The 2022 kickoff material cited 118,000 average daily bus riders in 2019 on 12 routes and said the B41 had 22,500 average daily riders [flatbush_ave_brooklyn_bus_priority_kick_off_june2022#p006_c0002]. By 2024 and 2025 decks, DOT described 132,000 daily or average daily riders on 12 routes, with buses as slow as 4 mph in some Flatbush contexts and below 4 mph in Downtown Brooklyn [flatbush_ave_cb8_jun2024#p004_c0005] [flatbush_ave_cb8_jun2025#p004_c0002].

## Safety And Street Context

Safety is a corridor-wide reason for intervention, but the evidence uses different reporting windows and geographies. A 2022 injury table for Flatbush Avenue from Tillary Street to Avenue V reported 3,070 total injuries, 155 severe injuries, 9 fatalities, and 164 killed-or-seriously-injured cases for 2016-2020 [flatbush_ave_brooklyn_bus_priority_kick_off_june2022#p014_c0004]. Later 2024 materials say Flatbush Avenue is a Vision Zero Priority Corridor with 11 Vision Zero Priority Intersections, located within a Vision Zero Priority Zone, and that the Downtown section ranked within the top 10% of high-crash corridors in Brooklyn, with 55 people killed or severely injured since 2019 [flatbush_ave_cb8_jun2024#p004_c0007]. A 2025 presentation gives another corridor safety snapshot, citing 140 people killed or severely injured within the past five years [flatbush_ave_bus_priority_sept2025#p004_c0002].

Operationally, the recurring problems are slow buses, congestion, and curb friction. The kickoff deck says AM peak buses moved slowly throughout the corridor, especially northbound between Atlantic Avenue and Cadman Plaza, with AM trips taking five minutes longer than overnight trips because of congestion and double-parking [flatbush_ave_brooklyn_bus_priority_kick_off_june2022#p012_c0002]. The PM-peak material emphasizes slow southbound trips from Empire Boulevard to Nostrand Avenue and northbound trips in Downtown Brooklyn, with PM trips 9-11 minutes longer than overnight and slowdowns especially in commercial areas [flatbush_ave_brooklyn_bus_priority_kick_off_june2022#p013_c0002].

## Corridor-Wide Bus Priority

The bus-priority toolkit for Flatbush is broader than the State Street-to-Grand Army Plaza phase. The Brooklyn Bus Network draft lists possible treatments including curbside, offset, or center-running bus lanes; a busway; transit-signal priority; queue jumps; and bus stop improvements [brooklyn_bus_network_draft_plan_without_route_profiles#p022_c0005]. By 2024, NYC DOT had already installed Transit Signal Priority at 37 Flatbush Avenue intersections in 2022, and planned three bus-only signals for summer 2024 at Ocean Avenue/Empire Boulevard, Parkside Avenue, and Church Avenue [flatbush_ave_cb8_jun2024#p008_c0005] [flatbush_ave_cb8_jun2024#p008_c0003].

The Northern Flatbush phase should be read as the first implemented slice of this larger corridor program. In 2024 and 2025, DOT said the current design round focused on the Downtown Brooklyn/Northern Flatbush section from Livingston Street to Grand Army Plaza because it had the slowest bus speeds and 69,000 average daily bus riders on six routes; the same source says improvements there would benefit riders throughout the corridor and that DOT planned to study treatments in other sections in future phases [flatbush_ave_cb8_jun2024#p010_c0002] [flatbush_ave_cb8_jun2025#p009_c0002]. The April 2026 briefing makes the phasing explicit: DOT split the center-running bus lane design into two phases, installed Livingston Street-to-State Street lanes in fall 2025, planned State Street-to-Grand Army Plaza lanes and boarding islands for spring 2026, and called the project the first part of a multi-phased effort to improve bus service on the entirety of Flatbush Avenue [flatbush_ave_bus_priority_mtp_briefing_apr2026#p004_c0002].

## Caveats

Scope language differs: Better Buses and the Brooklyn Bus Network draft say Tillary Street to Avenue V, while several outreach decks describe Tillary Street to Kings Plaza. Safety metrics should not be merged blindly: 164 KSI for 2016-2020, 55 KSI since 2019 for the Downtown section, and 140 KSI within the past five years appear to use different dates and scopes. The repo evidence reviewed here does not confirm post-briefing implementation status beyond the April 2026 plan. The materialized corridor record has aliases for the broad corridor and narrow State Street-to-Grand Army Plaza phase; no identity cleanup was performed.
