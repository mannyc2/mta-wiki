## Corridor Role

Hylan Boulevard is the Staten Island south- and east-shore corridor behind the S79 SBS implementation. Planning sources describe it as the only continuous north-south route on the south shore, with 28,000 vehicles on a typical weekday, long transit commutes, 16,000 daily S78/S79 local trips, and 15,000 daily trips on eight express routes. [2010_12_02_brt_hylan_open_house_slides#p004_c0004] The study corridor also included Richmond Avenue and transit anchors such as Staten Island Mall, Eltingville Transit Center, Staten Island Railway stations, the Verrazano-Narrows Bridge, and the Bay Ridge connection. [201106_hylan_slides#p005_c0005] [201106_hylan_slides#p022_c0007]

The corridor problem was not only bus speed. The study goals bundled traffic flow, bus travel time and reliability, transit access to Manhattan, Brooklyn, and Staten Island destinations, safety, and business access. [2010_12_02_brt_hylan_open_house_slides#p005_c0004] Public and CAC concerns likewise included congestion, driver and pedestrian safety, enforcement, slow buses, parking and delivery access, pedestrian crossing time, and missing sidewalks. [201106_hylan_slides#p006_c0004]

## Improvement Concept

By September 2011, the Hylan proposal placed a peak-period bus lane between Steuben Street and Midland Avenue, in effect weekday mornings and afternoons for S79 SBS plus local and express bus services; it also included four SBS stations, faster fare collection, and future real-time information. [201109_brt_hylan_proposed#p001_c0001] [201109_brt_hylan_proposed#p001_c0005] The same proposal paired the bus treatment with roadway and pedestrian work: resurfacing, median upgrades, pavement markings, pedestrian ramps, sidewalk reconstruction, and median refuges where feasible. [201109_brt_hylan_proposed#p001_c0007] Additional boards extended the treatment set to signal timing, TSP, a possible bus lay-by, pedestrian refuge islands, sidewalk gaps near bus stops, and seven Richmond Avenue SBS stations between Hylan Boulevard and Staten Island Mall. [201109_brt_hylan_proposed#p005_c0005] [201109_brt_hylan_proposed#p006_c0006] [201109_brt_hylan_proposed#p007_c0008]

By March 2012 the recommended plan focused the Hylan bus lanes on two miles between Lincoln Avenue and Steuben Street, described as the "Express Route to the Bridge," where local and express bus ridership was highest. [2012_03_15_brt_hylan_meeting_slides#p012_c0004] [2012_03_15_brt_hylan_meeting_slides#p012_c0005] The same plan called for fewer S79 stops and a more direct route, better public information, signal optimization, left-turn bay work, Richmond Avenue left-turn bays, and pedestrian safety enhancements. [2012_03_15_brt_hylan_meeting_slides#p007_c0004] The signal package included optimized timings across the project area, TSP along the S79 route, and an advance-signal pilot; the pedestrian package included nine Hylan/Richmond refuge locations, sidewalks to bus stops, sidewalk repair, curb ramps, and a median bus station at Richmond and Yukon Aves. [2012_03_15_brt_hylan_meeting_slides#p016_c0005] [2012_03_15_brt_hylan_meeting_slides#p019_c0005]

## Launch And Outcomes

S79 Select Bus Service began September 2, 2012. [2012_08_hylan_start_of_service_flyer#p001_c0001] The launch flyer described streamlined S79 service with fewer stops and a more direct route, Hylan bus lanes between Lincoln Avenue and Steuben Street, Richmond Avenue bus lanes, optimized signal timing, TSP coming in 2013, pedestrian refuge islands, sidewalk work, and Richmond Avenue left-turn bays. [2012_08_hylan_start_of_service_flyer#p002_c0003] [2012_08_hylan_start_of_service_flyer#p002_c0005] [2012_08_hylan_start_of_service_flyer#p002_c0007] [2012_08_hylan_start_of_service_flyer#p002_c0010] In operations, all S79 service became SBS, S59 and S78 retained local stops with added service, the SBS fare stayed the local bus fare, and express buses could also use the bus lanes. [2012_08_hylan_start_of_service_flyer#p002_c0013] The Hylan bus lanes were weekday peak-direction lanes: 6-9am northbound and 3-7pm southbound. [2012_08_hylan_start_of_service_flyer#p002_c0017] [2012_08_hylan_start_of_service_flyer#p002_c0018]

The 2014 final report describes the project as Select Bus Service along the S79 route, primarily along Hylan Boulevard, designed to improve the S79, other local and express bus routes, and conditions for drivers and pedestrians. [2014_hylan_blvd_final_report#p002_c0002] It reports 13-19% bus-speed improvement on the S79, S79 ridership growth of 10.8%, combined S59/S78/S79 growth of 6.6% while other Staten Island routes grew 1.2%, no substantial traffic-flow impact, and new pedestrian benefits including sidewalks and bus shelters. [2014_hylan_blvd_final_report#p002_c0003] End-to-end S79 travel time fell from 71 to 62 minutes northbound to Brooklyn and from 68 to 55 minutes southbound to Staten Island Mall; the report attributes savings to fewer stops, dedicated bus lanes, signal optimization, and more direct routing, while noting no off-board fare collection on this corridor. [2014_hylan_blvd_final_report#p014_c0005] For riders traveling on Hylan Boulevard between Richmond Avenue and Clove Road, reported S79 ride time fell from 54.6 to 46.5 minutes, an 8.1-minute reduction that the report ties to the two-mile curbside peak-period bus lanes. [2014_hylan_blvd_final_report#p019_c0003]

## Network Context

In the citywide BRT/SBS program, Hylan appears both as a Phase I BRT route and as a corridor in broader Phase II discussions. The Phase II introduction described Hylan Boulevard SBS as a planned faster connection from Staten Island's south shore to Brooklyn. [brt_intro_to_phase2#p018_c0005] [brt_intro_to_phase2#p038_c0003] Staten Island workshop feedback was mixed: Hylan received attention at all tables, but some participants thought a BRT route was unnecessary because of recent traffic-signal improvements, and participants suggested extensions to Bayonne and Richmond Avenue. [brt_phase2_outreach_rpt#p024_c0007]
