## Corridor Scope

The 79th Street corridor is the crosstown M79-SBS corridor in Manhattan, framed in 2025 capital-project material as "79th Street from Riverside Drive to FDR Drive." [79_st_riverside_dr_fdr_dr_cb8_apr2025#p001_c0002] The same deck describes it as a major transit corridor hosting M79-SBS, with 13,500 daily riders, connections to 4 subway lines and 16 bus routes including M15-SBS, and destinations including the American Museum of Natural History, the Metropolitan Museum of Art, Central Park, and Riverside Park. [79_st_riverside_dr_fdr_dr_cb8_apr2025#p004_c0003]

The route geometry is more complex than a straight 79th Street line: the 2020 after report maps M79 SBS from W 79 Street/West End Avenue across Central Park, then through East 86 Street, York Avenue, East 80 Street, and East 79 Street to East End Avenue. [brt_m79_after_report_feb2020#p004_c0002]

## Planning And Launch

The M79 bus corridor was identified as a potential Select Bus Service candidate in the 2009 "Bus Rapid Transit: Phase II Study." [brt_m79_cb8_mar2017#p006_c0002] M86 SBS then became the nearby model: the M86 progress report says DOT and MTA NYCT were planning to convert the M79 along 79th Street to SBS, with planning beginning in 2016 and a tentative spring 2017 launch. [m86_sbs_progress_report_2017#p011_c0010]

The October 2016 M86/M79 presentation described the routes as parallel crosstown routes with similar street designs and stop patterns, and said M79 analysis would investigate M86-style treatments to increase bus speeds and reliability, improve pedestrian and traffic safety, and improve the customer experience. [brt_m86_m79_cb8_oct2016#p011_c0002] That planning schedule moved from fall 2016 traffic analysis/design work, to winter 2017 draft-plan and design finalization, to spring 2017 SBS launch. [brt_m86_m79_cb8_oct2016#p018_c0003] [brt_m86_m79_cb8_oct2016#p018_c0005] [brt_m86_m79_cb8_oct2016#p018_c0007]

M79 Select Bus Service launched on May 21, 2017, replacing M79 Local service along 79th Street from FDR Drive to Riverside Drive. The 2020 after report describes the route as just over two miles and says the redesign brought SBS improvements to 12,500 daily bus riders. [brt_m79_after_report_feb2020#p002_c0003]

## Treatment Package

The 2017 Street Improvement Project package included 8 bus queue jump lanes, 1 full-block bus lane, signal timing improvements, off-board fare payment, real-time passenger information, and bus stop amenities/street furniture. [79_st_riverside_dr_fdr_dr_cb8_apr2025#p005_c0002] The after report separately summarizes 0.75 miles of bus lanes and 10 intersections with signal timing improvements. [brt_m79_after_report_feb2020#p008_c0003] [brt_m79_after_report_feb2020#p008_c0005] Off-board fare payment let riders pay before boarding and board through any of the three doors, reducing boarding queues and helping buses leave stops faster. [brt_m79_after_report_feb2020#p012_c0002]

The treatment geography split between West Side and East Side needs. West Side improvements included a new eastbound curbside bus lane to W 81st Street, protected turns and pedestrian crossings, and weekday mid-day school-bus layover space on Central Park West. [brt_m79_after_report_feb2020#p010_c0002] East Side treatments included a westbound bus queue jump signal and short segments of bus lanes to improve bus travel times. [brt_m79_after_report_feb2020#p011_c0002]

## Outcomes

The after report says M79 SBS was on average about 8% faster than previous M79 service, ridership was up about 9% one year after launch, car travel times decreased 4-8%, crashes decreased 19%, and rider satisfaction was 96% compared with 84% for pre-SBS service. [brt_m79_after_report_feb2020#p002_c0005] It also attributes improved bus travel times to off-board fare payment, bus lanes, and signal timing improvements, and says street redesigns plus signal timing improvements contributed to improved car travel times. [brt_m79_after_report_feb2020#p023_c0002] Dwell time at bus stops fell 21% on average across time periods, and the report says on-time performance and bus bunching improved in both directions. [brt_m79_after_report_feb2020#p016_c0002] [brt_m79_after_report_feb2020#p017_c0005]

By the April 2025 corridor presentation, M79-SBS performance was still being summarized as improved: bus bunching down 6%, bus dwell times down 21%, first-year M79-SBS ridership up 9%, corridor car travel times down 4% to 8%, average bus speeds up 8%, late buses down up to 45%, 2024 M79-SBS speed at 6.9 MPH versus 6.0 MPH for Manhattan, and 2024 on-time performance at 81.4% versus 69.5% for NYC. [79_st_riverside_dr_fdr_dr_cb8_apr2025#p007_c0002]

The same 2025 deck says all crash and injury categories are down since implementation, with pedestrian injuries down 63%, cyclist injuries down 17%, motor-vehicle crashes down 62%, and total incidents down 57%; its safety table is labeled as NYPD and NYS data. [79_st_riverside_dr_fdr_dr_cb8_apr2025#p008_c0002] [79_st_riverside_dr_fdr_dr_cb8_apr2025#p008_c0004] [79_st_riverside_dr_fdr_dr_cb8_apr2025#p008_c0005]

## Capital Follow-Up

The 79th Street SBS capital project is a follow-up to the 2017 launch, intended to further improve bus speeds, reliability, and pedestrian safety with permanent street elements: bus bulbs, curb extensions, pedestrian islands, and bus pads. [brt_m79_after_report_feb2020#p025_c0002]

As of the April 2025 CB8 presentation, the capital scope was to maintain the 2017 bus-lane alignment, bus-lane hours, and traffic signal changes; upgrade 15 bus stops, 14 bus pads, and 9 median tips/pedestrian refuges; and add 4 bus bulbs, 13 sidewalk extensions, and more than 20 street trees. [79_st_riverside_dr_fdr_dr_cb8_apr2025#p011_c0003] The CB8 deck estimated design completion in November 2025, construction start in June 2026, and construction completion in September 2029. [79_st_riverside_dr_fdr_dr_cb8_apr2025#p021_c0002]
