The corridor [[corridor:corridor_fifth-avenue|Fifth Avenue]] in Manhattan was identified as a Bus Priority/Hot Spot location within the NYC BRT Program, paired with Madison Avenue under the "Fifth Avenue/Madison Avenue Bus Priority" initiative [[cite:080421_brt_cb5_34th_street_fullboardpresentation#p003_c0008|BRT Program map showing Bus Priority locations]]. At the time of an April 2008 presentation to Manhattan Community Board 5, Fifth Avenue had an existing single bus lane from 86th Street to 34th Street [[cite:080421_brt_cb5_34th_street_fullboardpresentation#p006_c0003|Existing conditions list item]]. The proposed treatment called for converting Fifth Avenue to a double bus lane extended south to 23rd Street, painting bus lanes red, and adding overhead signage [[cite:080421_brt_cb5_34th_street_fullboardpresentation#p005_c0003|Key features list item]].

The corridor served 6 local bus routes with over 100,000 combined daily riders [[metric:metric_fifth-madison-6-local-routes|6 local routes]] [[metric:metric_fifth-madison-100k-daily-riders|over 100,000 daily riders]] [[cite:080421_brt_cb5_34th_street_fullboardpresentation#p010_c0003|Overall benefits list item]]. Projected travel time improvements of up to 20% were anticipated through the corridor [[metric:metric_fifth-madison-20pct-travel-time-improvement|up to 20% travel time improvement]] [[cite:080421_brt_cb5_34th_street_fullboardpresentation#p010_c0003|Overall benefits list item]]. The double-lane design was intended to let limited and express buses pass stopped local buses without entering general traffic, and reliability was expected to be substantially improved [[cite:080421_brt_cb5_34th_street_fullboardpresentation#p010_c0003|Overall benefits list item]]. Express bus routes from all outer boroughs also used the corridor [[cite:080421_brt_cb5_34th_street_fullboardpresentation#p010_c0003|Overall benefits list item]].
