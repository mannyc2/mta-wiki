M15-SBS - ABLE route

route_m15-sbs

Route Role

M15 SBS is the Select Bus Service route on First Avenue and Second Avenue in Manhattan. The 2011 progress report describes the underlying M15 as a critical Manhattan service connecting neighborhoods that were a long walk from the nearest subway station, and says NYCT and NYCDOT implemented M15 SBS on October 10, 2010. [201111_1st2nd_progress_report#p003_c0003] At launch, the SBS service replaced M15 Limited service. [2010_09_14_brt_1st2nd_cac5#p010_c0006]

Keep the route identity narrower than the broader First Avenue/Second Avenue SBS street project. The route operated uniformly between East 126th Street in Harlem and South Ferry, with a limited number of trips ending at Houston Street, while a September 2010 route diagram showed stations from South Ferry to 125 St/2 Av and 125 St/1 Av. [201111_1st2nd_progress_report#p010_c0005] [2010_09_14_brt_1st2nd_cac5#p010_c0011]

Service Design And Street Treatments

The service package combined proof-of-payment fare collection, new bus lanes and improved bus lane treatments, including offset bus lanes, with route simplification, new buses, improved scheduling, enhanced stations and customer information. [201111_1st2nd_progress_report#p003_c0004] A 2011 CAC presentation summarized the M15 SBS package as over 10 miles of dedicated bus lanes, NYPD and camera enforcement, off-board fare payment, revised station spacing and low-floor, three-door buses. [2011_04_27_brt_1st2nd_cac6#p003_c0006]

The route-supporting street design used both offset and curbside bus lanes. Offset lanes were in the second lane from the right curb and in effect at all times, preserving curbside parking or loading; curbside lanes were typically in effect from 7:00am-10:00am and 2:00pm-7:00pm, with 10:00am-2:00pm reserved for commercial loading in most locations. [201111_1st2nd_progress_report#p008_c0006] [201111_1st2nd_progress_report#p008_c0007] The same implementation also added bicycle paths and lanes, including parking-protected bicycle paths on parts of First and Second Avenues, and pedestrian safety islands on First Avenue from Houston Street to 34th Street and on Second Avenue from 34th Street to 23rd Street where street width allowed. [201111_1st2nd_progress_report#p013_c0004] [201111_1st2nd_progress_report#p014_c0003]

Fare Collection And Operations

Off-board fare collection directly addressed dwell time. The progress report says the former M15 Limited devoted 23% of travel time to passengers boarding and alighting, and that proof-of-payment let riders board through any of the three doors. [201111_1st2nd_progress_report#p009_c0003] [201111_1st2nd_progress_report#p009_c0004] The report credits the payment system with reducing M15 SBS dwell time by 40%, and says fare evasion changed from 6.7% before implementation to 4.2% in spring 2011. [201111_1st2nd_progress_report#p009_c0004] [201111_1st2nd_progress_report#p010_c0003]

Fare equipment was part of the route operation, not just a station amenity. M15 SBS stations received MetroCard fare collection machines and solar-powered coin fare collectors; most stations had two MetroCard machines and one coin machine, with more at high-volume stations such as First Avenue/14th Street. [201111_1st2nd_progress_report#p018_c0008] Through May 2011, availability was approximately 97% for MetroCard Fare Collectors and 99% for Coin Fare Collectors. [201111_1st2nd_progress_report#p018_c0008]

Performance Evidence

The 2011 progress report summarized early results as a 15-18% improvement in M15 travel time, a 9% increase in M15 ridership, a 21% reduction in traffic injuries in sections where full treatments were used, maintenance of traffic speeds and volume, and an 18-177% increase in cycling. [201111_1st2nd_progress_report#p003_c0006] Its running-time figure compared M15 Limited and SBS all-day averages from spring 2010 and winter 2011: southbound total running time changed from 85:37 on LTD to 73:55 on SBS, and northbound from 75:53 on LTD to 63:15 on SBS. [201111_1st2nd_progress_report#p018_c0003] [201111_1st2nd_progress_report#p018_c0004]

Wait assessment and rider satisfaction also improved in the source record. The progress report table labels the M15 Limited/SBS change from 2009 to 2011 as 10.7%, with SBS15 at 87.0% in the first half of 2011. [201111_1st2nd_progress_report#p018_c0005] [201111_1st2nd_progress_report#p018_c0006] Among riders interviewed on SBS, 99% were "satisfied" or "very satisfied"; among M15 Local riders, the figure was 90%. [201111_1st2nd_progress_report#p019_c0007]

Camera Enforcement And TSP

Camera enforcement appears in the M15 SBS record from launch-era materials forward. A September 2010 presentation said video cameras were coming in November 2010, and the 2011 progress report says fixed cameras were installed along First and Second Avenues shortly after SBS service began. [2010_09_14_brt_1st2nd_cac5#p021_c0006] [201111_1st2nd_progress_report#p011_c0003] By June 2011, more than 7,000 summonses per month were being issued by the camera system, and by July 2011 static pole-mounted cameras, bus-mounted cameras and mobile-unit cameras had issued over 30,000 violations. [201111_1st2nd_progress_report#p011_c0003] [201111_1st2nd_progress_report#p011_c0004]

Later camera sources connect the route to ABLE and ACE. A 2024 bus-lane camera report lists M15-SBS in the MTA on-bus mobile camera program route list and notes that mobile ABLE cameras were also in use through 2023. [bus_lane_camera_report_2024#p008_c0004] [bus_lane_camera_report_2024#p007_c0012] A 2022 meeting document lists ABLE pilot benefits including speed improvements of 5% above the borough average and a "30% decrease in collisions below borough average on M15 SBS." [meeting_doc_104791#p006_c0009] The staged ACE page updated May 15, 2026 lists "M15-SBS, 1 Av / 2 Av" as an ACE route and describes ACE as bus-mounted camera enforcement for bus lanes, double parking and bus stops. [mta_automated_camera_enforcement#p001_b0001]

Transit Signal Priority has its own timeline. A 2010 planning table showed Transit Signal Priority as a later M15 SBS feature below Houston St, and the 2011 progress report said active TSP south of Houston Street was expected by early 2012. [2010_03_10_brt_1st2nd_cb3#p004_c0003] [201111_1st2nd_progress_report#p012_c0007] The 2017 TSP report later says the new generation of TSP was implemented on "M15 SBS/ Lower Manhattan (2014)." [tsp_report_2017#p005_c0007] In the evaluated Lower Manhattan segment, the report shows after-TSP travel time decreases for South Ferry to East Houston Street of 18.2%, 4.7% and 16.0% across AM peak, midday and PM peak, and for East Houston to South Ferry of 13.6%, 9.2% and 14.4%. [tsp_report_2017#p007_c0005] [tsp_report_2017#p007_c0006] [tsp_report_2017#p007_c0008] [tsp_report_2017#p007_c0009]

Caveats And Open Questions

Service extent is expressed two ways in staged sources: East 126th Street to South Ferry with limited Houston Street trips, and a route diagram from South Ferry to 125 St/2 Av and 125 St/1 Av. [201111_1st2nd_progress_report#p010_c0005] [2010_09_14_brt_1st2nd_cac5#p010_c0011]

The 2017 TSP results apply to equipped Lower Manhattan TSP segments, not the full route; the report says segment data may not represent the entire bus corridor or current travel times. [tsp_report_2017#p006_c0005] A 2011 CAC presentation removed initial safety data because of a calculation error, so this page relies on the later 2011 progress report for the 21% traffic-injury reduction statement. [2011_04_27_brt_1st2nd_cac6#p026_c0011] [201111_1st2nd_progress_report#p003_c0006] Later route mentions in unrelated SBS outreach materials often use M15 SBS as a comparison, transfer, map, or tour reference; they were not used as primary evidence for this route draft.
