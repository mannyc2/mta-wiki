Corridor Role

Fordham Road is a corridor-level bus-priority case, with the Bx12 SBS as the historical anchor rather than the whole story. NYC DOT's Better Buses page defines the active Fordham Road study area as the corridor between the Major Deegan Expressway and Boston Road, and describes nine bus routes carrying 93,700 daily riders with connections to multiple subway lines and Metro-North. A 2021 workshop also broke the corridor into five discussion sections, from Major Deegan Expressway to Sedgwick Avenue, Sedgwick to Jerome, Jerome to Webster, Webster to Southern Boulevard, and Southern Boulevard to Boston Road. [better_buses#p001_b0001] [fordham_rd_major_deegan_expwy_boston_rd_jun2021#p013_c0002]

The Bx12 SBS history explains why Fordham Road became a reference point for later bus-priority work. DOT's BRT Phase II introduction says the city implemented its first phase of BRT service, called Select Bus Service, on Fordham Road in June 2008, and that the Bx12 SBS package was intended to improve speed, reliability, and passenger comfort while local service continued. The SBS full report separately identifies Fordham Road/Pelham Parkway as implemented in June 2008. [brt_intro_to_phase2#p020_c0003] [brt_routes_fullreport#p012_c0004] [brt_routes_fullreport#p012_c0005]

The corridor evidence goes beyond the route launch. A January 2021 Fordham Road CAB deck describes Fordham Road as a critical crosstown corridor for buses, general traffic, and trucks, with commercial hubs and major institutions. The same deck lists Bx9, Bx12 SBS/Local, Bx17, Bx22, Bx34, and Bee-Line 60-62 service on Fordham Road, with 93,700 average daily riders and 40,300 on the Bx12 SBS and local. It also identifies frequent curbside bus-lane blockages, increasing curb demand, and Fordham Road's Vision Zero priority-corridor status as reasons transit work needed to be paired with curb and safety changes. [fordham_rd_major_deegan_expwy_boston_rd_cab_jan2021#p010_c0002] [fordham_rd_major_deegan_expwy_boston_rd_cab_jan2021#p014_c0002] [fordham_rd_major_deegan_expwy_boston_rd_cab_jan2021#p015_c0002] [fordham_rd_major_deegan_expwy_boston_rd_cab_jan2021#p016_c0002]

The Fordham Road-Inwood materials widened the lens westward through the University Heights Bridge and 207th Street. In March 2022, DOT described the corridor as a major Bronx-Manhattan connection, with the Bx12 as the busiest Bronx bus route and 85,000 average daily bus riders on five routes. The same deck says Bx12 SBS speeds were slowest from Boston Road to Broadway/Isham Street westbound and from 207th Street/Broadway to Boston Road eastbound, with major congestion at the University Heights Bridge and between Webster Avenue and Grand Concourse. [fordham_rd_inwood_cab_mar2022#p007_c0002] [fordham_rd_inwood_cab_mar2022#p010_c0004]

By 2022 and 2023, DOT was studying three corridor design alternatives: offset bus lanes, an eastbound busway, and a two-way busway. The 2023 CB11 deck says the offset-lane alternative would convert existing curbside lanes to 24/7 offset lanes, the eastbound busway would restrict eastbound through traffic except buses and trucks, and the two-way busway would restrict both eastbound and westbound through traffic except buses and trucks. DOT later identified Alternative A, offset bus lanes, as the design to advance because it best balanced bus speeds, reliability, curb demand, and effects on north-south corridors; DOT also said the design would add roughly 150 parking/loading spaces, while acknowledging offset lanes could still be blocked by double parking. [fordham_rd_inwood_cb11_jun2023#p010_c0002] [fordham_rd_inwood_cb11_jun2023#p010_c0005] [fordham_rd_inwood_cb11_jun2023#p010_c0007] [fordham_rd_inwood_cb11_jun2023#p010_c0009] [fordham_rd_inwood_cb11_jun2023#p025_c0002]

Camera enforcement is corridor-relevant but not a stand-alone fix in the Fordham record. The 2024 bus lane camera report lists Fordham Road among Bronx stationary camera corridors and Bx12-SBS among ABLE routes through 2023. The June 2023 Fordham deck says Bx12 SBS buses began ABLE enforcement on November 18, 2022, and that nearly 16,000 tickets were issued on Fordham Road between Sedgwick Avenue and Southern Boulevard through April 21, 2023; it reported April 2023 buses 4.7% faster, but still slower than 2008 post-SBS speeds, and concluded enforcement alone had not restored prior speeds because blockages remained frequent. [bus_lane_camera_report_2024#p007_c0005] [bus_lane_camera_report_2024#p008_c0004] [fordham_rd_inwood_cb11_jun2023#p016_c0002] [fordham_rd_inwood_cb11_jun2023#p016_c0004] [fordham_rd_inwood_cb11_jun2023#p017_c0002]

The 2026 restart narrows the implementation focus to Sedgwick Avenue-Bronx River Parkway while preserving the broader Fordham Road corridor history. The May 2026 CB5 deck says the project restart aimed to improve bus speeds by at least 20%, states that buses on Fordham Road travel as slow as 5 mph, and proposes upgrading current lanes to offset bus lanes and widened curbside bus lanes. Its design summary converts many sub-standard curbside bus lanes into loading/parking lanes, turns the adjacent travel lane into a 24/7 offset bus lane between Hoffman Avenue and Sedgwick Avenue, widens curbside bus lanes where geometry or left-turn lanes constrain the design, and adds pedestrian safety/public-space treatments. [fordham_rd_sedgwick_ave_bronx_river_pkwy_cb5_may2026#p002_c0002] [fordham_rd_sedgwick_ave_bronx_river_pkwy_cb5_may2026#p003_c0002] [fordham_rd_sedgwick_ave_bronx_river_pkwy_cb5_may2026#p006_c0002]

For future updates, treat the 2026 material as a source-time proposal and implementation schedule unless later source evidence is added. The CB5 deck says final proposals would be presented in spring, implementation would begin in early summer, bus lanes were expected to be operational in late summer/fall, and post-implementation monitoring and engagement would follow. [fordham_rd_sedgwick_ave_bronx_river_pkwy_cb5_may2026#p011_c0003]

Caveats And Open Questions

Ridership scope varies by source: 93,700 daily riders on nine routes, 85,000 on five routes, and 130,000 affected riders in 2026 appear to use different scopes and timeframes. The canonical corridor merges Major Deegan-Boston Road, Fordham-Inwood, and Sedgwick-Bronx River Parkway evidence; scope should stay explicit by paragraph. ABLE/ACE naming is mixed: 2023 Fordham materials say ABLE, while 2026 and current MTA materials use ACE. No post-implementation Fordham Road performance results were found for the 2026 restart.
