34th Street, Manhattan

corridor_34th-st-busway

34th Street is a river-to-river Midtown corridor serving major destinations and transfer points, including Javits Center, Penn Station, Madison Square Garden, Macy's, the Empire State Building, and NYU Langone. The Alternatives Analysis described it as a corridor with high pedestrian volumes, local and commuter bus use, intercity bus activity, and truck-route functions. [34thstreet_aa_final#p014_c0002] [34thstreet_aa_final#p014_c0003]

The corridor has gone through several generations of bus-priority planning. In 2008, DOT/NYCT materials proposed repaving and restriping 34th Street between Third and Ninth avenues, improving river-to-river curb bus lanes with red lanes, overhead signage, and extended hours, and implementing those changes by the end of 2008. [080421_brt_cb5_34th_street_fullboardpresentation#p012_c0003] Later SBS-era materials describe the November 2011 M34/M34A SBS launch, upgrades from curbside to offset bus lanes, and eleven bus bulbs for faster boarding and pedestrian benefits. [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb4_jan2025#p008_c0002] A 2025 existing-conditions map shows a mixed bus-lane pattern: offset bus lanes in effect 24/7 and curbside bus lanes in effect Monday-Friday 7 a.m.-7 p.m. along the 12th Avenue-to-FDR corridor. [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb4_jan2025#p009_c0003]

The 2025 Enhanced Bus Priority materials frame the remaining problem as both transit and safety. DOT/MTA reported that bus speeds improved after SBS, but that M34/M34A SBS remained among the slowest routes, with average weekday peak speeds of 5.0 mph, speeds as low as 3 mph, and more than 750 weekday passenger-delay hours attributed to roadway congestion. [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb6_jun2025#p006_c0003] The same 2025 deck records community concerns about sidewalk crowding, long crossings, congestion, slow buses, long waits, loading needs, and requests for a 24/7 14th Street-style busway and pedestrian-safety elements. [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb6_jun2025#p008_c0002] Safety evidence in the January 2025 deck reports more than 450 injuries on 34th Street from 2019-2023 and a table total of 466 injuries, 32 severe injuries, 1 fatality, and 33 KSI across pedestrian, bicyclist, motor-vehicle, and other categories. [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb4_jan2025#p011_c0002] [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb4_jan2025#p011_c0004]

The 2025 draft proposal focuses the busway itself on the core Midtown segment: Third Avenue to Ninth Avenue in both directions. It would maintain existing bus lanes on the rest of the corridor, add pedestrian-safety improvements such as painted pedestrian spaces, hardened daylighting, and a concrete median island, and prioritize curb management for loading and deliveries. [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb6_jun2025#p010_c0002] The proposed access model allows buses, trucks, emergency vehicles, and Access-A-Ride to travel the full busway; other vehicles can use local access from side streets but must leave at the next required turn under posted signage, while loading, deliveries, and customer access are preserved. [34th_st_enhanced_bus_priority_cb6_jun2025#p011_c0002]

The public busway page and busways index describe the busway as a 1.1-mile segment between Ninth Avenue and Third Avenue, in effect daily from 6 a.m. to 10 p.m., serving over 28,000 daily riders on the M34/M34A and express buses. [busway_34thstreet#p001_b0001] [busways#p001_b0001] The staged busway brochure gives the directional limits as eastbound from Ninth Avenue to Third Avenue and westbound from Third Avenue to Ninth Avenue. [34th_st_busway#p002_c0002] [34th_st_busway#p002_c0003]
