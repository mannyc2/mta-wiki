Utica Av

corridor_utica-ave-st-johns-pl-church-ave-2013

Scope

This page is best used for the Utica Avenue street corridor studied and improved before the B46 SBS launch, especially the St. Johns Place-to-Church Avenue segment named in 2013 Community Board materials. The 2013 overview defines the focus as Utica Avenue from St. Johns Place to Church Avenue, calls out Eastern Parkway, Empire/Lefferts, and Church Avenue as key intersections, and gives the study goals as improving pedestrian and driver safety, improving bus travel times, and maintaining parking and curb access [2013_09_12_sbs_utica_cb8#p002_c0004].

Pre-SBS Corridor Study

The corridor work grew out of earlier Utica Avenue improvement planning rather than starting as a full SBS service plan. A September 2011 NYC DOT presentation to Community Boards 8, 9, and 17 described B46 bus issues, local loading requests near Eastern Parkway, and a Utica Avenue corridor study area from Carroll Street to Church Avenue; the same deck described the B46 as the second-busiest bus route in New York City, with more than 49,000 weekday passengers and congestion slowing buses approaching Eastern Parkway [2011_09_15_sbs_utica_cb8_9_17#p001_c0003] [2011_09_15_sbs_utica_cb8_9_17#p004_c0002] [2011_09_15_sbs_utica_cb8_9_17#p008_c0003] [2011_09_15_sbs_utica_cb8_9_17#p008_c0004].

By the April 2012 project update, DOT was presenting the corridor as a set of linked but distinct problem areas: St. Johns Place-to-Carroll Street, Empire Boulevard/East New York Avenue, and Carroll Street-to-Church Avenue. The same source records community feedback that the B46 was important local service, that pedestrian safety concerns were especially strong at Eastern Parkway, East New York Avenue, and Church Avenue, and that residents raised risky driving, parking supply, and enforcement concerns [2012_04_26_sbs_utica#p006_c0003] [2012_04_26_sbs_utica#p007_c0003] [2012_04_26_sbs_utica#p007_c0004].

The safety case for the corridor was explicit. The 2012 update lists safety needs from Carroll Street to Church Avenue and reports that 63% of vehicles exceeded the 30 mph speed limit, with 766 total crashes, 41 severe injuries, and 2 fatalities in 2006-2010; it also says Utica had among the highest motor-vehicle-crash injury rates in Brooklyn [2012_04_26_sbs_utica#p004_c0002].

Treatment Logic

The northern portion around St. Johns Place, Eastern Parkway, President Street, and Carroll Street was treated as a bus-delay and curb-access problem. Existing conditions identified traffic congestion as worst on approaches to Eastern Parkway, while the proposed improvement relocated the bus lane to the southbound approach, provided more parking between President and Carroll Streets, reduced southbound bus delays approaching Eastern Parkway, and added 19 peak-period parking spaces [2012_04_26_sbs_utica#p009_c0004] [2012_04_26_sbs_utica#p010_c0007].

South of Carroll Street, the 2013 concepts were broader corridor design tools: convert one travel lane to an offset bus lane to reduce speeding, maximize parking, and benefit bus service; add left-turn bays at some intersections while prohibiting left turns elsewhere; implement Transit Signal Priority; and optimize signal timings. Church Avenue was singled out as the busiest single-route bus-to-bus transfer in the city, a very high crash location, and a proposed location for offset bus lanes, bus bulbs, left-turn prohibition, and high-volume traffic management [2013_09_24_sbs_utica_cb9#p011_c0005] [2013_09_24_sbs_utica_cb9#p012_c0006] [2013_09_24_sbs_utica_cb9#p017_c0003].

2014 Bus Priority Layer

The 2014 bus-priority package described the St. Johns-to-Church corridor as 1.3 miles of offset and curbside bus lanes, paired with left-turn restrictions to improve pedestrian safety and left-turn bays to maintain traffic flow. It also described parking and curb-management changes: 19 added spaces near Eastern Parkway, 22 removed spaces along the southern part of the corridor, and new loading zones for businesses [2014_09_16_brt_uticaavenue_cac1_presentation#p012_c0003] [2014_09_16_brt_uticaavenue_cac1_presentation#p012_c0005] [2014_09_16_brt_uticaavenue_cac1_presentation#p012_c0007].

A later B46/Utica newsletter records the street-design timeline as a Summer 2014 bus lane project implemented from St. Johns Place to Church Avenue. That same timeline separates this implemented bus-lane project from later work south of Church Avenue, where it lists developed and finalized street design changes plus new markings and curb regulations [brt_uticaave_winter_spring_newsletter_2016#p003_c0005] [brt_uticaave_winter_spring_newsletter_2016#p003_c0012] [brt_uticaave_winter_spring_newsletter_2016#p003_c0017] [brt_uticaave_winter_spring_newsletter_2016#p003_c0018] [brt_uticaave_winter_spring_newsletter_2016#p003_c0019] [brt_uticaave_winter_spring_newsletter_2016#p003_c0020].

The early performance evidence belongs to the bus-priority corridor, not necessarily to the later full SBS route. A November 2014 Utica presentation reports that, in sections where a bus lane was installed, B46 Limited and local travel times decreased 8% to 15%, while vehicle travel times decreased 20% to 25% in the peak direction during AM and PM peaks [2014_11_brt_uticaave_pw1#p014_c0003] [2014_11_brt_uticaave_pw1#p014_c0004].

Relationship To B46 SBS And Malcolm X/Utica

The full B46 SBS project is a later service layer built on top of this corridor groundwork. The 2014 CAC deck says the Utica Avenue B46 corridor had been identified as a potential SBS candidate in the 2009 BRT Phase II Study, and then describes Utica Avenue SBS as building on the existing Bus Priority and Safety Project to upgrade B46 Limited to B46 SBS with off-board fare collection, additional bus lanes, TSP, reliability-focused service planning, and SBS station capital improvements [2014_09_16_brt_uticaavenue_cac1_presentation#p010_c0002] [2014_09_16_brt_uticaavenue_cac1_presentation#p014_c0002] [2014_09_16_brt_uticaavenue_cac1_presentation#p014_c0003].

The later Malcolm X/Utica materials should be treated as follow-up evidence, not as the identity of this St. Johns-to-Church corridor. The March 2020 deck says the B46 had been upgraded to SBS in 2016 and to articulated buses in January 2020, identifies Malcolm X Boulevard/Utica Avenue as a 2019 Better Buses priority project, and focuses its proposal on a southbound curbside bus lane from Chauncey Street to Fulton Street [malcolm_x_blvd_utica_ave_mar2020#p004_c0002] [malcolm_x_blvd_utica_ave_mar2020#p006_c0002] [malcolm_x_blvd_utica_ave_mar2020#p014_c0002].

Caveats

The target corridor record merges aliases for Carroll-to-Church, St. Johns-to-Church, B46 SBS planning, 2016 follow-up, and 2020 Malcolm X/Utica material; this page keeps the narrower St. Johns/Church corridor separate in prose. Exact block-by-block as-built limits and enforcement hours for the 2014 bus lanes are not fully resolved from the checked evidence. Later SBS route operations and ABLE/ACE camera enforcement are better handled on the B46/Utica SBS route page.
