The Flushing-Jamaica Corridor was first identified in the initial NYC BRT study (2006) and the Bus Rapid Transit Phase II report (2009), and was later listed as a BRT Phase II candidate corridor Flushing to Jamaica corridor identified in initial NYC BRT study (2006) and BRT Phase II report (2009). Early planning materials described the corridor as characterized by "long and slow bus trips" Characterized by "long and slow bus trips". It serves multiple bus routes: Q20A/B and Q44 on Main Street, Q25 and Q34 on Kissena Boulevard/Parsons Boulevard, and Q65 on 164th Street Flushing-Jamaica Corridor served by Q20A/B, Q44 on Main St, Q25, Q34 on Kissena Blvd/Parsons Blvd, Q65 on 164 St.

Main Street carries roughly 42,000 daily riders 42000 riders on the Q44 (Jamaica to West Farms) and Q20A/B (Jamaica to College Point) routes 42,000 daily riders on. Parsons Boulevard/Kissena Boulevard carry roughly 26,000 daily riders 26000 riders on the Q25 (Jamaica to College Point) and Q34 (Jamaica to Whitestone) routes 26,000 daily riders on. The Q44 was prioritized for a Select Bus Service upgrade based on its ridership—over 29,000 daily—its role as a key connection between Queens and the Bronx, and the opportunity for on-street bus priority on Main Street Q44 was prioritized for SBS upgrade based on Ridership – Over 29,000 per day on Q44.

The BRT Phase II study (2009) listed the Flushing-Jamaica Corridor as one of 16 potential future BRT corridors, appearing in the candidate table as candidate 14 Table of Routes Short List for Next Select Bus Service Route ... 14 Flushing-Jamaica Corridor. Public outreach for the Phase II effort included seven workshops across the city and an online survey, with results summarized in a June 2010 public input report Public input was the second key component of the BRT Phase II effort The workshop and survey participants provided DOT and NYCT with a wealth of feedback.
