116th Street Study Area, Manhattan

corridor_116th-street

116th Street Study Area, Manhattan is a bus priority and pedestrian safety study corridor in Manhattan led by NYC DOT under the Better Buses program, covering 116th Street, Manhattan Avenue, Pleasant Avenue, and Morningside Avenue Better Buses program. The full study area spans approximately 2.2 miles between 114th Street and 113th Street, while the CB10-focused portion covers about 1.1 miles along 116th Street, Manhattan Avenue, and Morningside Avenue study area 2.2 miles CB10 study area 1.1 miles. The corridor serves over 65,000 daily bus passengers across 10 bus routes—M3, M7, M102, M116, BxM1, BxM6, BxM7, BxM8, BxM9, and BxM11—and provides critical crosstown connections to the 2, 3, 6, B, and C subway lines 10 bus routes, subway connections.

Congestion on the corridor produces severe bus speed and reliability issues. M116 buses travel less than 4 mph 4 mph in some segments during the morning peak M116 bus speeds under 4 mph. Weekday congestion causes 785 hours of delay 785 hours to M116 passengers daily 785 hours daily delay.

Community engagement through Community Boards 9, 10, and 11 identified a vision for the corridor: make bus service fast, reliable, and on-time; improve safety for walking and driving; ensure residential access; add commercial vehicle loading; and explore programmable public space vision items. Key challenges include traffic congestion, double parking, and vehicle circulation needs challenges. NYC DOT presented existing conditions to CB9 in February 2025 and CB11 in March 2025, followed by draft proposals to CB10 and CB11 in May 2025, and a draft proposal to CB9 in June 2025, with implementation tentatively planned for summer 2025 project timeline through summer 2025.

Proposed treatments across the corridor include offset bus lanes (four blocks on 116th Street from Manhattan Avenue to 5th Avenue, maintaining parking), a daytime curbside bus lane (7a-7p on 116th Street between 5th Avenue and Madison Avenue), seven left turn lanes at key intersections, four concrete pedestrian islands with bollards, 33 painted pedestrian spaces (nine islands and 24 curb extensions providing hardened daylighting at 11 intersections), signalized mid-block crossings between Frederick Douglass Boulevard and 5th Avenue, a pedestrian space created by repurposing a slip lane on Morningside Avenue and Manhattan Avenue, and additional amenities such as bus boarders, benches, leaning bars, queue jump signals, and curb extensions proposed treatments list project summary metrics.
